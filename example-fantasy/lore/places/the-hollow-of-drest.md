# The Hollow of Drest — Village of the First Trial

## Overview

Drest is a farming village of perhaps two hundred people built in a shallow valley three days north of Valdenmere. The valley is surrounded on three sides by dense, old-growth woodland. The fourth side opens toward the road. The village is tidy, well-maintained, and has not changed significantly in forty years.

The name comes from the old word for bowl or hollow — the valley's shape. Locals simply call it Drest. The full name is used on maps and by outsiders.

## The Forest

The woodland surrounding Drest on three sides is called, locally, the Deep. It is not, by any objective measure, a particularly dangerous forest. There are deer, a few wolves in the outer margins, birds, and the usual understory. The trees are old and the canopy is heavy enough to block most light, which makes the interior dim even in daylight. This contributes to the atmosphere.

At the center of the Deep, in the area the villagers call the Heart, is a natural stone outcropping — a flat-topped rock formation about knee height, wide enough for several people to stand on. This is where the Shard of Stone rests. The ground around it is untouched. Nothing has disturbed this location in a very long time.

The creature that once terrorized the village — which is called, in village accounts, the Drest-thing — left no physical traces. Whether this is because it was supernatural in nature or simply because four decades of growth have covered whatever marks it left is unclear. The distinction does not matter much for practical purposes.

## The Village

The village has two rows of houses along a central track, a mill, a well, a common meeting hall, and a small shrine near the offering post at the treeline. The offering post is a carved wooden pillar, twice the height of a person, with hooks for hanging cloth and a shelf for food items. It is maintained. Fresh offerings appear on it every month.

The inn is called the Low Hearth. It has eight rooms and a stable. It is run by the Keswick family, who have run it for three generations.

The village is governed by a council of five elders, the eldest of whom is the great-grandchild of the original hunter who drove the creature off. The council does not vote on major decisions so much as reach quiet consensus in private and then announce it. The process works efficiently and produces outcomes that always, somehow, favor the council's own interests.

## The Fear

The fear of the Deep is embedded in everything the village does without appearing explicitly in any formal rule. Children are warned at a young age. The warnings come from parents, from grandparents, from older siblings, from stories told at communal meals. None of it is written down. It does not need to be.

The offering schedule is treated as civic maintenance — the same category as clearing the drainage channels or checking the mill wheel. It is simply what one does. People who fail to contribute to the monthly offering are not punished formally. They are simply looked at differently. Community pressure is a more reliable enforcement mechanism than fines.

The few people in Drest who privately doubt the threat have learned to doubt privately. The innkeeper's daughter, who went to the treeline once on a dare and saw nothing, has told one person. She chose carefully.

## Aftermath

What Maren does with the offerings — removing them from the post and returning them — does not resolve the situation in Drest. The elders will put new offerings up the following week and the village will continue as before. The three companions are not positioned to reform a community they passed through in two days.

But the innkeeper's daughter will remember that someone came through and told her she was asking the right questions. That memory will do its work in its own time.
