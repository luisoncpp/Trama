# The Osswall — Fortress of the Demon King

## Overview

The Osswall is a fortress complex at the northern edge of the kingdom's recognized territory, built against a cliff face at the point where the Verge — the wide, featureless lowland that marks the border — meets the first rise of the northern rock shelf. It is the largest structure any of the three companions have seen, though none of them will say so in each other's hearing.

The name appears in historical records predating the current regime. The structure existed before the prince was possessed. What it was originally built for, and by whom, is not recorded in any source the companions have access to. The demon did not build it. It occupied it.

## The Verge

The Verge is approximately fifteen kilometers of low, dark grassland between the northern mountain road and the Osswall. It is not hostile in any specific way. It does not have unusual weather, dangerous terrain, or threatening wildlife. It is simply a landscape that does not seem to want to be crossed.

The sky over the Verge is slightly lower-looking than everywhere else, as if the cloud layer sits closer to the ground. Sound carries strangely — footsteps seem louder than they should, voices seem slightly wrong in pitch. These effects are not supernatural. They are the result of flat terrain, heavy air, and the absence of trees to absorb and scatter sound. The Verge has a reputation for being haunted that is entirely based on these acoustic properties.

The walk across it takes most of a day.

## The Fortress

The Osswall's outer walls are black granite, approximately twelve meters high, with towers at irregular intervals. The gate is a wide arch that is open and has been open for years — the portcullis mechanism failed some time in the demon's occupation and was never repaired, because the demon has no interest in keeping people out. It has an interest in being where it is and doing what it does. Visitors, in its experience, do not survive long enough to be a problem.

The interior of the fortress is organized around a central courtyard. The structures around it include a great hall, a wing of residential chambers, a storage level, and the throne room, which occupies the highest point of the main building — reached by a single wide staircase from the courtyard.

The corridors are lit by a bioluminescent substance that grows on the walls — a grey-green lichen that gives off enough light to see by and that the demon has apparently encouraged. The effect is cold and even and deeply unpleasant. The human inhabitants of the fortress — servants, or people who have been in service long enough that the word servant has lost its usual meaning — move through this light with the same smooth, mechanical quality. They are not controlled in the way the monks are not controlled: they simply have no remaining investment in their own preferences. This took years. The demon is patient.

## The Throne Room

The throne room is large and cold and has windows on three sides that look north, east, and west over the Verge. The throne itself is a heavy stone seat that was clearly built for someone else — it has been modified in small ways over the years, none of them improvements. It faces the door.

The Demon King receives visitors here. He sits at the throne and does not rise. He is economical with words and completely indifferent to the impression he makes. He does not perform power. He simply has it, and the room already knows it.

The relics are placed, after he takes them, on a stone table to the left of the throne. They are not destroyed or hidden. They simply sit there. He cannot use them and knows it. He intends to determine, in time, what prevents him from using them. He does not expect the answer to come from three people in his dungeon.

## The Dungeon

The dungeon is below the courtyard level — a series of cells cut into the rock rather than built, accessed by a narrow stair with no rail. The cell that holds the three companions is on the second level down: stone floor, stone walls, a door of heavy timber and iron with a sliding bolt on the outside, and a grated window near the ceiling that admits grey light from a shaft cut through the rock to the surface.

The shaft is too narrow for a person. It is wide enough for sound, for cold air, and for the very distant impression that the world above continues to exist.

The cell is where the story becomes legible. It is the only room in the Osswall where no one is performing, no one is maintaining a useful illusion, and no one is waiting to be told what to think.

## After the Resolution

Once the prince is freed and the demon expelled, the Osswall's inhabitants — those who have been in service long enough to have lost themselves — do not immediately recover. Some of them will. Some of them are too far in. This question is not resolved in the text, but it is not ignored either. The three companions, leaving, pass through the courtyard and see these people standing in the sudden absence of the thing that organized their days, and the sight is not simple.

The fortress itself will need to be addressed by the kingdom. It is not discussed in the final chapter. It is a problem for later, and the story acknowledges this by not pretending it has been solved.
