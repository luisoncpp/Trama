# Solen's Crossing — Town of the Second Trial

## Overview

Solen's Crossing is a prosperous river town of perhaps two thousand people, built on both banks of the Solen river at the point where the current slows enough for a bridge to be practical. The bridge — the great stone bridge that is the town's oldest and most celebrated structure — connects the two halves of the settlement and carries most of the north-south trade route across the river.

The town is old by regional standards and knows it. The architecture in the older quarters is heavy and ornamental in a way that suggests someone, at some point, wanted people to know they had money. The newer districts are more practical.

## The Bridge and the Festival

The bridge is genuinely impressive — wide enough for two carts to pass, with low carved parapets and a cobbled surface that has been repaired so many times no original stone remains. At its center stands the statue of Aldric the Warden, facing north, one hand raised in the direction of the formerly hostile territory. The statue is replaced every fifty years with a newer, slightly larger version. The current statue is forty-three years old.

The Aldric Festival runs for three days in midsummer and draws visitors from the surrounding towns. It includes a market, a theatrical performance of the founding myth, competitive games, and a ceremony at the bridge in which the current mayor pours river water from a ceremonial jug and says words that nobody fully remembers from one year to the next but which are understood to be important.

The festival is the most economically significant event in the town calendar. It would be difficult to overstate how much the town's identity depends on it.

## The Archive

The Archive of Solen occupies a two-story building on the west bank, three streets back from the river. It holds the official historical records of the town and the surrounding region: property transfers, census records, trade agreements, legal judgments, and the official commemorative texts relating to the town's founding.

The building's ground floor is maintained and accessible to researchers with appropriate credentials. The archivist, a meticulous man named Pell, has worked there for nineteen years and knows every document on the ground floor in considerable detail.

The lower level is accessed through a door at the back of the ground floor stacks. The door is unlocked. It is simply behind a shelf unit that requires moving. Pell has never moved it. His predecessor never mentioned it to him. The level's existence is documented on the building's original plans, which are in a file in Pell's own office, which he has never had reason to open.

The lower level contains the actual founding records. It also contains the page from an older text — the one with the marginal annotation — which was placed there by someone who understood what it said and wanted it to survive in more than one location. The identity of that person is not established in the text. The question of who they were, and how they understood the legend well enough to plant the annotation, is left open.

## Atmosphere

Solen's Crossing is comfortable in a way that comes from not asking too many questions. The town is not malicious. The people are friendly, generous during the festival, and genuinely proud of their history. They simply have no particular interest in whether their history is accurate. It feels true, it functions as a shared story, and it brings in visitors for three days every summer. These seem like sufficient reasons.

Cael, who has performed the Aldric legend for money, finds the town difficult to enjoy. Oswin finds it professionally fascinating and personally troubling. Maren buys good bread from a stall by the bridge and describes it as the best thing she has eaten since they left the capital, which she means as a simple observation and which Cael takes as a reproach.
