# The Monastery of the Bound Hand — Site of the Third Trial

## Overview

The Monastery of the Bound Hand sits on a wide natural ledge in the northern mountain range, approximately half a day's climb above the last waystation on the mountain road. It is visible from the lowlands on clear days as a long, pale line against the grey rock — low buildings, a single bell tower, walls that were built for permanence rather than grandeur.

The monastery was founded roughly two centuries ago by a scholar named Weld, who had spent forty years in secular intellectual life and concluded, near the end of it, that individual reasoning was the primary source of human suffering. His founding text argues, at considerable length, that the capacity to choose independently is not a gift but a burden, and that the highest form of life is one in which that burden is voluntarily transferred to a structure worthy of bearing it.

The text is rigorous, internally consistent, and profoundly wrong in ways that took more than a century for anyone inside the monastery to begin to notice.

## The Community

The monastery houses approximately sixty monks at any given time, along with a small number of permanent lay staff. It accepts novices from families throughout the region, drawing particularly from scholarly households who consider the Bound Hand tradition intellectually prestigious. Oswin's scriptorium maintains a formal relationship with the monastery and considers it the senior institution.

The monks are not unhappy, by the most accurate measure of the word. They experience genuine peace. Conflict is rare because independent desire is rare. The daily rhythm is steady, productive, and predictable. The scriptorium output is exceptional — the monastery produces the most accurate copies of historical texts in the region, partly because the monks have no opinion about the content and copy with complete fidelity.

This last fact is significant. The master copy of the legend of the three relics, held in the monastery's manuscript room, contains the marginal annotation precisely because the copyist who originally transcribed it reproduced the annotation faithfully without editorial judgment. A scholar working in a different tradition might have dismissed the addition as a later corruption. The monk who copied it had no framework for that decision and simply included it. The annotation survived.

## Rector Vael

The current Abbot, Rector Vael, has held the position for twenty-six years. He is a gentle, intelligent man of about seventy who genuinely believes what the monastery teaches and has lived it honestly for most of his adult life.

He is also, without knowing it, in a position structurally identical to the demon's: a will at the center of a system that depends on no other will asserting itself. He is not cruel. He does not need to be. The structure does the work.

When Oswin sits across from him and says something true from his own judgment, Vael's response — _the first honest thing anyone has said in this room in eleven years_ — is not sarcasm. He means it. He has been waiting for it for eleven years and has not known he was waiting. The moment does something to him that the text does not describe directly. What he does next is his own affair.

## The Manuscript Room

The manuscript room is at the east end of the monastery's main building. It is temperature-controlled through a system of stone channels that circulate cool air from the mountain, designed by the founder and maintained ever since. The room holds the monastery's archival collection: several hundred volumes, all copied on-site, organized by subject and date.

The master copy of the legend occupies a place of particular distinction — a dedicated case, enclosed glass, documented provenance. It is the original from which every copy in circulation descends. Oswin has handled copies of copies of copies. Seeing the original is, for him, the equivalent of a musician hearing a song performed by the person who wrote it.

The marginal annotation, seen in the original, is in a hand that is clearly not the primary copyist's. The ink is different — slightly darker, with a different sheen. It was added perhaps fifty years after the original text was transcribed. The identity of the person who added it is unknown to the monastery. The monks noted the discrepancy when they catalogued the manuscript a century ago, recorded it as a minor irregularity, and moved on.
