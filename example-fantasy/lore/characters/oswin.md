# Oswin — The Novice Scribe

## ![img_0](res/lore_characters_oswin_0.png)

## At a Glance

Oswin is a young man of nineteen sent by his scriptorium to document the quest. He has read more than almost anyone his age and understood, at the time of his departure, far less than he believed.

## Background

Oswin entered the scriptorium at fourteen as a copy apprentice. He spent five years in careful, precise transcription — copying legal documents, property records, religious texts, and old chronicles. He has excellent handwriting. He has copied the same legendary texts three or four times each, including the original account of the three relics and the trials required to reach them.

When the king announced the quest and Cael was summoned, the abbot of the scriptorium saw an opportunity. A documented firsthand account of a legendary quest would be historically significant. He sent Oswin as the official chronicler with a mandate to observe, record, and return safely.

Oswin arrived at the palace gates the morning of Cael's departure carrying a waterproof satchel, two travel journals, four stoppered ink pots, and a very thorough plan for organizing his notes.

## Personality

Oswin is earnest, nervous, and more capable than he appears. He talks too much when he is anxious — which is frequently — and tends to narrate what is happening in a low murmur, as if writing in his head. He apologizes for things that are not his fault. He asks questions at inconvenient moments because the question becomes unbearable the moment he thinks of it.

He is also, once the initial nerves quiet, genuinely observant and rigorous. He does not guess when he can verify. He does not claim to remember something he is not sure of. He distinguishes carefully between what he saw, what he was told, and what he inferred — a habit that turns out to matter considerably when the group is trying to reconstruct the meaning of the legend in a prison cell.

He knows the old texts. Not in the way a scholar interprets them, but in the way a copyist knows them: word for word, line for line, including the strange margins, the unusual punctuation, and the passages that never quite parsed into plain sense. He had always assumed those odd sections were transcription errors from earlier generations. In the dungeon, he realizes they were not.

## Role in the Story

Oswin is the group's connection to the written record. He has the text. He knows what the original legend actually says, not what people remember it says, and those two things turn out to be different in several important ways.

The revelation in the dungeon is collaborative, but Oswin's contribution is specific: there is a marginal annotation in the original manuscript — a tiny note in a different hand — that he copied faithfully without understanding. In the cell, when Cael describes the structure of the ballad and Maren pushes them to be precise, Oswin realizes the annotation is an interpretive key. He can recite it from memory because he copied it four times. He just never knew what it meant.

His personal arc is about the difference between knowing something and understanding it. He enters the story full of recorded information and exits it having learned that experience is what turns records into knowledge. He also discovers, for the first time, that he is capable of choosing to do something difficult — not because it was assigned to him, but because he decided it mattered.

## Key Detail

Oswin's travel journals are meticulous. He records dates, weather, distances walked, names of people encountered, and exact quotations. He also draws small maps in the margins. At several points in the story, information in his journals turns out to be the only reliable source of something the group has forgotten or gotten wrong in memory.
