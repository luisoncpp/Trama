# Chapter 1 — The Summons

**Arc:** The Call
**Dramatic question:** Why would a bard with no belief in prophecy agree to go
on a prophesied quest?

---

## Opening

The Gilded Post inn, Valdenmere. Evening. Cael is midway through a ballad about
a merchant who accidentally married a river spirit. The common room is half-full.
The audience is moderately amused. Two soldiers step through the door and ask
for him by name.

He finishes the verse before he stops. This is either professionalism or
stubbornness. Possibly both.

---

## Scenes

**The escort to the palace.**
The soldiers are not unfriendly, but they offer no information. The walk
through Valdenmere at night gives Cael — and the reader — a first impression
of the city: large, self-assured, a place that has decided what is true and
does not revisit the question. The palace is impressive at a distance. Up close
it is older and more patched than it looks from the street.

**The receiving room.**
Small, lit by two lamps, with one advisor seated against the wall. Aldren is
behind a table. The document is already prepared. The meeting is efficient.
Aldren lays out the situation without drama: the Demon King, the prophecy, the
relics, Cael's role. He seems entirely unsurprised by Cael's skepticism and
does not argue with it. Belief, he says, is not required. Action is.

Cael reads the document before signing. This takes a few minutes. Nobody
rushes him. He signs.

**The question.**
As Cael stands to leave, Aldren asks whether Cael knows a song called
*The Weight of Three*. Cael says yes, though he rarely performs it — the
melody is awkward and the verses don't resolve in a way audiences find
satisfying. Aldren nods and thanks him. The advisor writes something down.
Cael does not think about this again for several chapters.

**That night.**
Cael in his room at the Gilded Post, the last night he'll pay for it. He
has a purse, a letter of passage, a formal blessing he did not particularly
want, and an assignment he does not believe in. He goes through his belongings
with the pragmatic efficiency of someone who has packed in a hurry many times.
He does not write a letter home. There is no one at home to write to.

He lies awake for a while. He is not afraid exactly. He is aware that
something has changed and he is not sure yet whether the change is interesting
or simply inconvenient.

---

## Ending Beat

Morning. The north gate. Cael passes through it with his instrument on his
back and his knife at his belt. The gate guards do not look up from their game.
Fifty people leave every morning. He is one of them.

The city does not see him go.

---

## What This Chapter Establishes

- Cael's character: observant, unhurried, professionally skeptical of stories
  that have been shaped for an audience.
- Aldren: efficient, unexpectedly human in small ways, watching Cael leave
  with something that could be mistaken for hope.
- The world: Valdenmere is a city of managed certainty. The official story
  about the Demon King and the dead prince is embedded in the city the way
  the old cart tracks are embedded in the streets. Nobody thinks about it.

## Plant

Aldren's specific question about *The Weight of Three*. It is one line. It
lands quietly. The reader should notice it only on a second reading, when they
already know what it means.
