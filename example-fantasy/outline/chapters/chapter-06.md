# Chapter 6 — The Lower Level

**Arc:** The Archive of Solen
**Dramatic question:** What do you do with a truth when there is no clear
person to give it to?

---

## Opening

The morning after the festival crowd. The square is quieter. They go to the
Archive as soon as it opens.

---

## Scenes

**Finding the lower level.**
The official map marks the annex as a sealed room in the northwest corner of
the basement. Oswin had noted, yesterday, that the building's external
dimensions suggest a basement larger than the ground-floor plan accounts for.
The discrepancy is small but consistent.

They find the access without elaborate effort: a door behind a shelf unit that
has not been moved in some years, judging by the dust on the floor, but which
is not locked, not blocked, and not structurally damaged in any way visible
from the outside. Pell is occupied with a researcher on the ground floor. They
are quiet about it.

The lower level is a long, dry corridor with shelves from floor to ceiling on
both sides. The documents are organized by date, labeled in a hand that has
been down here more recently than the dust would suggest — someone has been
maintaining this room. Not recently, but not neglected either.

**Oswin reads.**
He begins at the oldest materials and moves forward. He does not speak for
approximately twenty minutes. Cael sits on the floor with his back against a
shelf. Maren reads a few pages, understands the relevant implication within
about three minutes, and waits.

Then Oswin says, in a very quiet voice: the founding account is not accurate.
He begins listing specific discrepancies. The army. The battle. Aldric's role.
The bridge construction. He is precise and somewhat relentless, the way he
gets when the documentary evidence is unambiguous.

**The Still Mirror.**
It is at the far end of the room, on a shelf at eye height, still wrapped in
cloth that has mostly lost its original form. Cael finds it while Oswin is
still listing discrepancies. He unwraps it and turns it over.

The black glass surface shows him the shelves behind him — every document,
every record, three centuries of evidence sitting untouched while the story
above accumulated festivals and statues and a very well-paid theatrical company.
It does not show him his face. It only looks backward.

He stands there for a long moment.

Maren asks what he sees.

He says: *what was already here.*

This is not the answer she asked for, but it is the honest one. She accepts it.

What Cael is not saying, and will not say: he performed the Aldric legend
himself. He shaped it, improved it, made it better for audiences. He had
never wondered about the room below the archive because the room below the
archive was not part of the performance. What he performed became what the
actor in the square performs, which becomes what the pamphlet says, which
becomes what everyone knows. He had always told himself this was simply
how stories worked. He is finding it harder to find that thought comfortable.

He wraps the mirror carefully. He puts it away.

**The question that doesn't have an answer.**
On the way out, Oswin asks whether they should tell anyone about what
they found. The records are here. The truth is documented. Someone should
presumably do something with this.

Maren says: tell who, and what do you expect them to do with it.

Oswin thinks about this. Cael thinks about it for longer, and less productively,
because he is also thinking about something else.

---

## Ending Beat

Before leaving the lower level, Oswin notices a single page that does not
belong. It is filed between two property dispute records from roughly three
centuries ago. The page is from an older text entirely — the paper is different,
the hand is different, and the content is a short verse in a dialect that
predates Solen's founding by at least a century.

Oswin recognizes it. It is the marginal annotation from the legend manuscript —
the one he has copied four times, the one he has never fully parsed. He does
not know why it is here, in a commercial archive, among property records. He
copies it into his journal without stopping to think about why, because copying
things without stopping to think about why is what he does.

He draws a box around the copy. Second box in the journal, same content as
the first. On his way back upstairs he almost mentions it to the others, then
does not. He is not ready to explain what he does not understand.

---

## What This Chapter Establishes

- The trial's resolution: the truth is found. It changes Cael specifically —
  not in a way he announces, but in a way the reader can watch settling.
- Maren's role in this arc: she asks the pragmatic question that neither of
  the others will ask. Not cynically — honestly. Change is not as simple as
  discovering a truth.
- The annotation's second appearance: the mystery deepens just when the
  chapter seemed to resolve.

## Payoff / Plant

- **Payoff:** Pell's over-long answer was the tell. The hidden room was
  what he was protecting. Oswin's note in Chapter 5 pays off here.
- **Plant:** The annotation found in a wrong place, in a wrong archive,
  filed deliberately among records three hundred years old. Someone planted
  it here. The question of who is not yet asked. It will be asked, and not
  answered, in Chapter 10.
