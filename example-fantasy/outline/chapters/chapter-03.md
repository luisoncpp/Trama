# Chapter 3 — Drest

**Arc:** The Hollow of Drest
**Dramatic question:** What does a community look like when its fear has become
a management tool?

---

## Opening

Three days north of Valdenmere. The road descends into a valley. The trees
press in close on either side before the village appears: tidy houses, a
central track, a mill. It looks peaceful. It takes a few minutes to identify
why it doesn't feel that way.

---

## Scenes

**Arrival.**
They reach the village in the early evening. The atmosphere is orderly in a
way that has a faint edge to it — doors shuttered a little earlier than the
light requires, people moving with a slight purposefulness toward indoors.
The inn is called the Low Hearth. The innkeeper is polite and efficient and
answers questions about the road north without pausing, and answers questions
about the forest without answering them.

**The offering post.**
Oswin notices it immediately: a carved wooden pillar at the treeline, twice
the height of a person, hung with cloth strips and a shelf of food items.
He asks the innkeeper about it with the enthusiasm of someone who finds
folk practice professionally interesting. The innkeeper gives him two sentences.
A farmer at the bar changes the subject. An elder at a corner table finishes
his ale and moves to their table uninvited.

**The elder's account.**
His name is Dreskin. He is the current head of the overseer's family — a
detail he mentions, in passing, near the beginning. He gives them the full
account of the creature: the disappearances forty years ago, the hunters
who drove it deep into the forest, the ongoing threat it represents, the
offerings that have kept the village safe since. The account is detailed,
grave, and structured. Specific names. Specific dates. The emotional weight
distributed exactly where it needs to be to produce the desired response.

Cael listens without expression. He has performed stories designed to produce
a specific emotional response. He knows what they sound like from the inside:
the named victims, the carefully placed doubt-closers, the way the narrative
creates a room with no exits. He says nothing.

**The innkeeper's daughter.**
Her name is Betta. She is fourteen and moves through the common room with
the practiced invisibility of someone who has been doing this work since she
was small enough to go unnoticed. She does not look frightened of the creature.
She looks bored by fear — the specific exhausted expression of someone who has
been afraid of the same thing her entire life and started resenting it without
having words for the resentment.

Maren sees this. She makes no comment in the common room.

**The kitchen.**
Later, after the common room has emptied for curfew, Maren finds Betta washing
dishes. They talk. Betta is careful at first — testing whether this is a person
who repeats things. Maren passes the test without trying to pass it, which is
the only way to pass it.

Betta says she went to the treeline once on a dare, when she was eleven. She
saw nothing. She says she sometimes thinks about whether anyone has actually
checked the forest in forty years. She says she would never say this to someone
who would carry it back to the elders.

Maren says: you're asking the right questions.

---

## Ending Beat

Betta dries the last dish. Neither of them says anything else worth recording.
Maren goes upstairs.

In the hallway she passes the room where Cael and Oswin are talking in low
voices. She can hear Cael describing, quietly, what a story looks like when
someone has shaped it to keep people from leaving. She does not stop to listen.
She already understood.

She goes to sleep. The village keeps its curfew outside. The offering post
stands at the treeline with its cloth and its food, doing what it has always done.

---

## What This Chapter Establishes

- The first trial's actual mechanism: the fear is real in experience but false
  in source. There is no creature. There is only a system of stories that
  serves people who benefit from the fear.
- How Cael's skill reads: his ability to recognize a manufactured story is
  introduced here as a practical tool, not just a character quirk.
- Maren and Betta: a quiet, important moment. Maren is, instinctively, the
  person who tells someone their doubt is legitimate.

## Plant

The elder mentions that the family of the original overseer still holds the
position. Cael notes this. Oswin writes it down. It is not followed up in the
text explicitly — but it is the piece that makes the village's management of
fear legible as a structure rather than a superstition.
