# Chapter 5 — The Festival

**Arc:** The Archive of Solen
**Dramatic question:** What does it feel like to stand in the middle of a
story you have told, knowing it isn't true?

---

## Opening

Solen's Crossing. They arrive during the Aldric Festival week. The town is
decorated. The bridge is hung with colored cloth. A theatrical company is
performing in the main square to an audience of perhaps three hundred people.

The atmosphere is warm, prosperous, and completely convinced of itself.

---

## Scenes

**Cael and the performance.**
He stops at the edge of the crowd before the others have gotten their
bearings. The company is staging the founding myth — Aldric the Warden,
the invaders driven back, the bridge built in heroic defiance. The lead
actor is competent. The choreography is enthusiastic. The crowd responds
at every beat.

Cael watches with the particular attention of someone evaluating a competitor.
He knows this story. He has performed a version of it himself — two years
ago, in a town a week south of here, to a receptive and well-paying audience.
He gave it a stronger ending and a better melody for the bridge-building
sequence.

He does not tell the others this.

**The Archive.**
Oswin has been given the location of the Archive from his scriptorium's
records and leads the group there directly. The building is two stories,
organized, clearly maintained by someone who cares about the materials. The
archivist on duty — a precise man named Pell, nineteen years in the post —
welcomes researchers, provides the official index, and gives them a thorough
orientation of the ground floor.

Oswin works his way through the index methodically and then asks about the
structural annex marked on the official map. Pell says it's been sealed for
years due to structural concerns and there is really nothing down there of
research interest.

Oswin writes this answer down verbatim in his journal. Beside it he writes:
*his answer to my question about the annex was approximately twice as long
as his answers to every other question.* He draws no conclusions. He simply
records what happened.

**The festival crowd.**
Maren, who has no particular interest in the archive or the performance, spends
the afternoon in the market. She buys bread from a stall near the bridge and
describes it, later, as the best thing she has eaten since leaving the capital.
She also speaks at length with three separate market vendors and knows more
about the town's actual history of disputes and grievances than any official
text would provide. She does not connect this to the quest. She is simply
interested in how places actually work.

**Cael, later, at the tavern.**
That evening Cael returns to the festival square alone. He finds the theatrical
company's lead actor at a tavern after the performance — tired, pleased with
the show, ready to have a drink paid for by someone who wants to talk about
his craft. Cael is good at this. Two hours of professional conversation,
complimentary and specific.

Then Cael asks whether the company ever researched the actual founding records
before writing the script.

The actor looks at him as if this is a mildly strange question. He says they
used the official commemorative pamphlet. It has everything you need, the
story's well-established, why would anyone look further? Everyone knows how
Solen was founded.

Cael agrees that everyone does and thanks him for the conversation.

---

## Ending Beat

He walks back to the inn through the festival street. Colored lanterns are
still lit. The crowd is smaller now but still celebrating. The bridge is hung
with cloth honoring a man who mediated a commercial dispute and is remembered
as a warrior.

Cael has performed this story. For money. To people who left the performance
believing it.

He walks through the lantern light in a worse mood than he left the inn in,
and does not, for once, have anything to say about it.

---

## What This Chapter Establishes

- The second trial's mechanism: the false story is not hidden or suppressed,
  it is celebrated. The truth is sitting in the building they can see from
  the bridge, untouched for three hundred years.
- Cael's specific discomfort: he is implicated in this particular kind of
  deception. He is not an outside observer. He performed the Aldric legend
  himself and made money from it.
- Oswin's method demonstrated again: Pell's over-long answer is the tell,
  and Oswin records it without editorializing.

## Plant

Cael has not told the others he performed the Aldric legend. He will not tell
them in the next chapter either. It sits in the chapter as something the reader
knows and the characters don't address, which is its own form of discomfort.
