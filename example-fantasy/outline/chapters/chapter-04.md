# Chapter 4 — The Forest

**Arc:** The Hollow of Drest
**Dramatic question:** What does it cost to walk toward the thing you've been
told is going to destroy you?

---

## Opening

The morning after the elder's account. Cael has spent part of the night
mapping what he knows. The map does not flatter the village's story.

---

## Scenes

**The geographic problem.**
Cael explains it to Oswin over breakfast, quietly enough that no one at the
neighboring tables can follow. The most feared area of the forest — where
Dreskin placed the creature's current territory, where the most recent
"disappearances" supposedly occurred — is in the direction farthest from the
village. Not north, where the creature was originally driven, but east, away
from the road. Farthest from the one route outsiders could use to investigate.

An animal that actually lived near the village would approach from the nearest
dense cover. The fear has been pointed the wrong direction. It has been aimed
at the place nobody has any reason to walk toward except to look.

Oswin follows the logic, makes a note, and asks how sure Cael is. Cael says
he is not sure enough to bet his life on it, but he is sure enough to walk.
Maren, arriving mid-sentence, asks what they're walking toward and listens
to the summary. She says: tonight, then.

**The wait.**
The chapter takes its time with this. The group spends the rest of the day
at the inn with the knowledge of what they are doing that evening. Oswin
catalogs the offering post in careful detail from a distance. Cael listens
to everything being said in the common room and learns, from two separate
conversations, that no one has been taken in at least eight years — the most
recent story involves a farmhand who "went too close" and was found confused
the next day, which could mean anything. Maren bakes two small loaves using
the inn's oven and the flour from her cart. She does not explain why.

**Into the forest.**
Full dark. No moon to speak of. The three of them cross the treeline east
of the village, past the offering post, into the Deep.

The forest is not dangerous. This is the trial. There is nothing here but
trees, undergrowth, the sounds animals make when they are not being hunted,
and the complete absence of any of the specific things the elder described
in such careful detail. The fear is entirely in their heads, planted by
years of other people's careful narrative, and it works regardless.

Oswin recites botanical facts under his breath. Not for information — he
knows these facts, he doesn't need to recite them — but because his voice
going through familiar material gives him something to hold onto. Cael hums
fragments of songs for the same reason: not to calm himself but to have
something to follow from verse to verse when the next shadow moves and his
body wants to stop.

Maren walks ahead of both of them.

**The outcropping.**
The Shard of Stone is exactly where the geography of the fear pointed — the
center of the supposedly most dangerous territory, at a flat-topped rock
formation that the forest has grown up around without touching. The stone
is pale grey, about the size of a fist, and sits on the rock as if it has
always been there.

Maren reaches it first. She crouches and looks at it for a moment before
picking it up. The moment she holds it, she understands its weight: not the
physical weight, though that is disproportionate, but the correspondence. The
stone is as heavy as the things she has been carrying as practicality for two
years. Fear of the empty house. Fear of arriving somewhere new and being
nobody. Fear that if she stopped moving, stopped being useful, stopped
filling the silence with work, she would have to sit with what was actually
happening inside her.

She has called all of this good sense. She has called it forward motion.
She has not called it what it is.

She picks up the stone. It is very heavy. She holds it in both hands for a
long moment, then puts it in her bag and stands up.

**The return.**
The walk back is easier. Not because the fear is gone — it isn't — but
because they are going toward something rather than away, and that is a
different quality of movement.

At the treeline, Maren stops at the offering post. She takes everything off
it — the cloth, the food, the small objects — and puts them in her pack.
She does not explain why. Cael and Oswin watch and say nothing. It is not
a question of whether to ask. There is nothing to ask.

---

## Ending Beat

Before dawn. They leave the village while it is still dark, before the
elders can find the empty post and before anyone has reason to look at them
differently.

On the road north, Cael hums a few bars of *The Weight of Three*. He is
not thinking about it. It is simply what surfaces when something in him has
settled.

Oswin, walking behind him, writes a note in his journal by the grey light
coming into the sky: *the bard hums when something has been resolved.* He
marks it with an asterisk and goes back to watching the road.

---

## What This Chapter Establishes

- The trial's nature: not danger but the recognition that the fear is the
  only obstacle, and choosing to move anyway.
- Maren's arc step: the stone shows her what she's been carrying. She doesn't
  say this aloud to the others. She doesn't need to. The reader has been given
  enough to understand it.
- The group's coordination: they go together without a formal plan. The trust
  is not stated. It is enacted.

## Payoff / Plant

- **Payoff:** The geographic analysis (Cael's ability to read false stories
  in action for the first time). Established in Chapter 3, used here.
- **Plant:** The second instance of the hum, recorded by Oswin. The first
  was in Chapter 2 (the recited lines). The third will come on the approach
  to the Osswall.
