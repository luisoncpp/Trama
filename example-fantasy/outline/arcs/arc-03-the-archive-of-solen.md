# Arc 3 — The Archive of Solen

**Chapters:** 5–6
**Trial:** False Stories
**Relic:** The Still Mirror — a small disc of polished black glass that shows
the reflection of what is behind the viewer, not in front. It cannot be made
to show the present. It only looks backward.
**Companion most tested:** Cael

---

## Background: What Solen Is

Solen's Crossing is a prosperous river town that has built its entire civic
identity around a founding myth. Three hundred years ago, according to the
official account, a hero named Aldric the Warden single-handedly drove off an
army of foreign invaders, forged a peace treaty at swordpoint, and built the
great stone bridge across the Solen river — the bridge that made the town's
trade possible.

The town commemorates Aldric every year with a festival. His statue stands at
the bridge's center. His name is on the town gate. Children learn his story
before they learn to read.

The actual records, preserved in the Archive of Solen on the west bank, tell
a different story. There was no army. There was a protracted dispute between
two merchant families over bridge rights and river access that destabilized the
region for two decades. Aldric was a mediator hired by both families — competent
and genuinely useful, but not a warrior and not a hero in any dramatic sense.
The bridge was built by a collective of forty-three workers over three years,
most of them from outside the town, none of them commemorated anywhere. The
peace treaty was a commercial contract.

The merchant families who benefited from this arrangement commissioned the
Aldric legend a generation later. It was better for business than the truth.
It gave the town a story it could be proud of and, more practically, it erased
any competing claim on the bridge.

The Archive still contains the original documents. The town has not destroyed
them — largely because most people in Solen's Crossing do not know the Archive
has a lower level, and those who do assume it contains nothing important.

The Still Mirror is in that lower level, in the room the official town map of
the Archive marks as a storage annex that was sealed due to structural damage.
The room is not sealed. The map is wrong. The room is where the original
records are kept, along with everything else the town found inconvenient.

---

## Chapter 5 — The Town

The group arrives in Solen's Crossing during the week of the Aldric Festival.
The town is decorated. There are performances in the square. A theatrical
company is staging a dramatic version of the founding myth involving a great
deal of shouting and sword choreography.

Cael watches the performance with the professional attentiveness of someone
evaluating a competitor. He knows the story. He has performed a version of it
himself, in a town two years back, to a very receptive audience. He made good
money on it. He does not tell the others this.

Oswin, following the relic's supposed location to the Archive, requests access
to the historical records. The archivist on duty is polite and helpful. He
provides them with the official index, the official map, and a very thorough
guided orientation to the Archive's ground floor. He does not mention the
lower level. When Oswin asks about the structural annex marked on the map,
the archivist says it's been sealed for years and there's really nothing of
interest down there.

Oswin writes this down verbatim, then writes beside it: *the archivist's answer
to my question about the annex was twice as long as his answers to every other
question.*

That night, Cael goes back to the festival square alone. He finds the theatrical
company's lead actor at a tavern, buys him a drink, and spends two hours asking
detailed questions about where the company researched the script. The actor
says they used the official commemorative pamphlet. Cael asks if anyone ever
looked into the original records. The actor laughs and says why would they —
everyone knows the story.

Cael walks back to the inn in the dark. He is in a worse mood than when he left.

## Chapter 6 — The Trial

They enter the Archive the following morning and find the lower level without
much difficulty — the official map is wrong, but the building's actual
architecture makes the space obvious to anyone looking at the external
proportions and then counting the internal rooms.

The lower level is a long, dry chamber lined with shelves. The documents are
intact, organized, and clearly old. Oswin begins reading immediately and
does not speak for twenty minutes. Then he says, in a very quiet voice, that
the founding account is not accurate. He begins listing specific discrepancies.

The Still Mirror is on a shelf at the far end of the room, sitting in a cloth
wrapping that has mostly rotted away. When Cael picks it up and turns it over,
he sees, in its black surface, the reflection of the shelves behind him —
every document, every record, every piece of evidence that has been sitting here
undisturbed for three centuries.

He stands there for a long moment.

Maren asks what he sees. He says: what was already here.

This is where the arc's real weight lands. Cael has spent his professional life
telling people the stories they paid to hear. He has always told himself it was
harmless — performance, not deception, because everyone knew it was a show.
But standing in this room, surrounded by the unperformed version of a story he
has told for money, he cannot make that argument feel true anymore. The stories
he performs do not stay in the hall where he performs them. They become what
people believe. And what people believe shapes what they protect and what they
destroy.

He does not say this out loud. He wraps the mirror carefully and puts it away.

On the way out, Oswin asks if they should tell anyone about what they found.
Maren says: tell who, and what do you expect them to do with it. Oswin thinks
about this for the rest of the day. Cael thinks about it for longer.

> **Plant:** Among the documents in the lower level, Oswin finds a single page
> from an older text — clearly not native to this archive, clearly placed here
> rather than stored here — that contains a short verse he recognizes. It is
> the marginal annotation from the legend manuscript. He copies it into his
> journal without yet understanding why it is here, in a commercial archive,
> among property records three hundred years old.

---

## Arc Function

This arc is Cael's turning point. His detachment — his professional habit of
performing stories without caring whether they are true — is exposed here as
something more than a quirk. It is a way of avoiding responsibility. The trial
forces him to hold the mirror (literally and otherwise) to that habit.

The arc also advances Oswin's thread. The annotation he finds here does not
make sense yet. It will not make sense for three more arcs. But it is now in
his journal alongside the lines Cael recited by the fire in Chapter 2. The
pieces are accumulating, waiting for the dungeon.

Maren's role in this arc is largely to be the person who does not flinch from
the uncomfortable questions the other two are circling. Her question — *tell
who, and what do you expect them to do with it* — is not cynical. It is
honest. Change is not as simple as revealing a truth, and she knows this
from experience.
