# Arc 1 — The Call

**Chapters:** 1–2
**Central question:** Who are these three people, and why would any of them
agree to go on this quest?

---

## Chapter 1 — The Summons

Cael is performing at a mid-grade inn in Valdenmere when the king's guard
arrives. The inn is only half-full. He is in the middle of a ballad about a
merchant who accidentally married a river spirit, which the audience finds
moderately amusing. Then two soldiers step through the door and ask for him by
name.

The summons is not dramatic. The king does not meet him in the great hall with
torches and ceremony. He meets him in a small receiving room, seated behind a
table, with one advisor present and a sealed document ready to sign. The king
explains the situation efficiently: the Demon King occupies a fortress at the
northern border, the ancient prophecy names a traveler of songs as the one
who must seek the three relics, and Cael is the traveler the prophecy describes.

Cael has, of course, performed the legend of the three relics many times. He
knows the structure of the story. He does not believe a word of it. He says as
much, politely. The king nods as if this is not unexpected and explains that
belief is not required — only action. He will be given a letter of passage,
provisions, a small purse, and the formal blessing of the crown.

Cael signs. He is not sure why. He is two coins away from being thrown out of
his lodging and the purse is not small. But he is also, in some part of himself
he has not examined in a long time, curious.

The king watches him go with an expression that could be mistaken for hope.

> **Plant:** The king asks Cael one oddly specific question during the meeting —
> whether Cael knows the old ballad called *The Weight of Three*. Cael says he
> does, though he never performs it; it's too strange to hold an audience. The
> king simply nods and thanks him. Cael forgets the exchange almost immediately.
> The reader should not.

## Chapter 2 — The Road

Cael leaves Valdenmere on foot the following morning. He does not own a horse.
He follows the north road, which is also the road toward the Hollow of Drest
and, further on, toward the other trial sites.

He meets Maren two hours out of the city. She has a cart loaded with a brick
oven, two sacks of flour, a bag of tools, and a very organized approach to
where everything is stored. She is heading to a town called Millhaven, roughly
in the same direction. She assesses Cael, calculates that he is unlikely to
cause problems, and offers him a place on the cart in exchange for help if the
road becomes difficult. She is not warm about the offer. She is practical.

Oswin catches up to them by midday, slightly out of breath, carrying a satchel
large enough to suggest he has never packed lightly in his life. He was
dispatched by his abbot the morning of Cael's departure and spent the first
hour convinced he was going in the wrong direction. He presents a formal letter
from the scriptorium explaining his role as official chronicler and asks
whether the quest has begun yet or if he has missed anything.

Maren says it has not. Cael says he is not sure it has either.

The three travel together for the rest of the day. Maren does most of the
practical organizing. Oswin writes in his journal with one hand while eating
with the other. Cael watches both of them and says very little.

That night, camped at a crossroads inn, the first real conversation happens.
Oswin begins summarizing what he knows about the three relics from the original
manuscript. Maren listens, then asks which version he is quoting, because she
once heard a completely different account from a storyteller at her town
market. Oswin says there is only one original manuscript. Maren says that
storyteller was very confident.

Cael, without looking up from his bowl, says that there are at least four
distinct versions of the legend in circulation and that the oldest one he knows
— which he learned as a song — doesn't include the relics at all. It ends
differently. He recites two lines of it from memory. Oswin writes them down
immediately, then stares at what he has written.

Nobody says anything for a moment. The fire pops.

> **Plant:** The two lines Cael recites are the opening of *The Weight of Three*.
> They sound like pleasant but somewhat meaningless verse. Oswin writes them in
> his journal and draws a small box around them, noting that they do not appear
> in the manuscript version. He is not sure why this bothers him.

## Arc Function

This arc establishes character voices and dynamics before the group has any
reason to trust each other. Cael is detached but attentive. Maren is competent
and private. Oswin is earnest and more perceptive than he appears.

It also plants two seeds that will bloom in the dungeon:
- The king's specific interest in *The Weight of Three*.
- The discrepancy between Cael's version of the legend and the written text.

The arc ends with the group moving. They are not yet a team. They are three
people going the same direction who have agreed, provisionally, not to make
things harder for each other.
