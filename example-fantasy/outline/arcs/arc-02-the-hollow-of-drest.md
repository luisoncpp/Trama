# Arc 2 — The Hollow of Drest

**Chapters:** 3–4
**Trial:** Fear
**Relic:** The Shard of Stone — a piece of grey rock that feels heavier than
its size suggests. It grows lighter when set down freely rather than hidden away.
**Companion most tested:** Maren

---

## Background: What the Hollow Is

The Hollow of Drest is a valley village three days north of Valdenmere, nestled
in a low bowl of land surrounded by dense woodland. Forty years ago, something
came out of those woods at night. People disappeared. Animals were found dead.
The village was genuinely terrorized for almost two years before a group of
hunters drove the creature deep into the forest and it was never seen again.

The creature is dead. It died in that forest, probably within the first decade
after the hunters drove it off. No one has been taken in forty years. But the
village does not know this, or more precisely: the village leadership knows it,
and the rest of the village does not.

The elders of Drest maintain the fear. Every month, offerings are left at the
treeline — food, woven cloth, small objects. The entire village keeps curfew.
No one goes into the forest after dusk. Children are raised with the creature
as a present, living threat. Those who ask too many questions are reminded of
specific instances when people who were careless were taken. The stories are
detailed and authoritative and at least half of them are fabricated.

Fear keeps the village organized. It prevents arguments about land, about
succession, about the old divisions between founding families. A frightened
community is a cohesive community. The elders believe they are protecting
everyone. Some of them have been doing it so long they have forgotten it is a
choice.

The relic — the Shard of Stone — is in the forest. It rests on a natural rock
outcropping at the center of the old creature's territory. The forest is
overgrown but passable. There is no danger there. The only danger is the
belief in danger.

## Chapter 3 — The Village

The group arrives at Drest in the evening. The village feels tense in a way
that takes a moment to identify. People are polite, but they move quickly
indoors before dark. Shutters close. The inn's common room empties early.

Oswin notices the offering post at the treeline and begins asking questions
with the detached enthusiasm of someone who finds folklore professionally
interesting. The innkeeper gives short answers. A farmer at the bar changes
the subject. An elder, overhearing, sits down uninvited and gives them the
full account of the creature — detailed, grave, clearly well-rehearsed.

Cael listens to the elder's story and recognizes its structure. He has
performed similar stories. He knows what a story for keeping people afraid
sounds like from the inside: the specific details, the named victims, the
careful way it leaves no room for doubt. He says nothing that evening.

Maren, meanwhile, has been watching the innkeeper's daughter, who is maybe
fourteen, move around the common room. The girl doesn't look frightened. She
looks bored by fear — the particular exhausted expression of someone who
has been afraid of the same thing her entire life and has started to resent it
without having the words for why.

That night, Maren finds the girl lingering in the kitchen. They talk. The girl
says she once went to the treeline on a dare and saw nothing. She says she
sometimes wonders if anyone has actually checked. She says she'd never say this
to anyone who would repeat it.

Maren tells her she's asking the right questions.

> **Plant:** The elder mentions, almost in passing, that the original hunters
> who drove the creature off were led by a man who later became the village's
> first overseer. His family still holds that position. Cael files this detail
> away. Oswin writes it down.

## Chapter 4 — The Trial

Cael maps what he knows. The offering schedule. The curfew. The specific areas
the elders describe as most dangerous. He and Oswin study the account against
any geographic logic and conclude that the most feared part of the forest is
the direction farthest from the village — not the direction an animal would
naturally approach from. The fear has been pointed the wrong way.

They go into the forest the following night.

The trial is not a monster. It is the walk. The forest is dark and unfamiliar
and the fear is entirely in their own minds, planted by decades of story.
Every sound becomes the creature. Every shadow moves. Oswin recites botanical
facts under his breath to manage his anxiety. Cael hums fragments of songs,
not to comfort himself, but because moving from verse to verse gives him
something to follow.

Maren walks ahead of both of them.

She is the one who finds the relic. She almost doesn't pick it up. The stone
sits on its outcropping looking entirely unremarkable, and for a moment she
understands exactly why it's here and what it represents. She has been carrying
things this heavy for two years. Fear of the empty house. Fear of losing the
stall. Fear of arriving in a new town and being no one. She has called it
practicality. She has called it good sense. She has not called it what it is.

She picks up the stone. It is very heavy. She holds it in both hands for a
long moment, then puts it into her bag.

On the way back, they pass the offering post. Maren stops and takes everything
off it — the cloth, the food, the small objects — and puts them into her pack
to return to whoever left them. She doesn't say why. Cael and Oswin don't ask.

The next morning they leave before the elders can react to the missing
offerings.

## Arc Function

This arc introduces the mechanics of the trials. Importantly, the trial is not
dramatic in a conventional sense — no combat, no puzzle, no locked door. The
challenge is recognizing what you are actually afraid of and choosing to walk
toward it anyway.

Maren's arc takes a significant step here. She has been useful, competent,
and closed. The trial doesn't open her immediately, but it makes a crack.

The arc also establishes that Cael's observational instincts are genuinely
useful, not just a personality quirk. His ability to hear a false story is
what tells them the danger isn't real. He is, without yet realizing it,
practicing the skill he will need at the end.

> **Foreshadow:** Cael, on the return walk, quietly hums a few bars of
> *The Weight of Three*. He is not thinking about it. He doesn't mention it.
> Oswin, walking behind him, catches the melody and writes it in his journal
> later that night as a note to himself: *the bard hums when something has been
> resolved.*
