# Arc 6 — The Truth

**Chapters:** 9–10
**Central question:** What were the relics actually for, and what does knowing
the answer require them to do?

---

## Chapter 9 — The Dungeon

The cell is cold, lit by a single grated window near the ceiling, and has
nothing in it except stone and the three of them. Maren has bread in her bag.
She divides it without comment.

They begin with the question she asked at the end of the previous chapter:
what did they miss. They are methodical about it, which is Oswin's influence —
he opens his journal and starts from the beginning of the journey. What they
saw. What they were told. What they assumed.

The inventory takes most of the first day.

By evening, four things sit unresolved:

1. The king asked Cael specifically about *The Weight of Three* before the
   quest began. He never explained why.
2. The structure of that ballad — which Cael has been humming in fragments
   throughout the journey — is unlike any other song in the tradition. Its
   verses describe three objects, but the objects don't do anything. They are
   held, not used. They illuminate something the holder was already carrying.
3. The marginal annotation in the manuscript, which Oswin copied faithfully,
   which appeared again in Solen's archive, which was added to the original in
   a different hand than the text itself.
4. The Iron Key is warm. It has been warm since Oswin picked it up. Nothing
   in the cold of the dungeon has changed that.

Cael starts humming the ballad properly — not in fragments but from the
beginning. Maren listens. Oswin writes.

The ballad has three verses. Each verse describes a person who is carrying
something too heavy to name: an old grief, a story they kept telling even
after they stopped believing it, and a vow they made for someone else's
reasons. In each verse, the person encounters one of three objects. The object
does not change what they carry. It shows them what they have been carrying.
That is all. The verse ends. The person is different.

There is a fourth verse, which Cael has always considered an awkward appendix
— the melody shifts, the rhyme scheme changes, the language is older and harder.
He sings it now, slowly.

The fourth verse describes what happens when someone who has seen all three
objects speaks the name of the one who is trapped inside the one who is ruling.
Not the demon's name. The prince's name. Spoken not at the demon but through
it, aimed at whoever is still alive underneath.

Oswin finds the annotation in his journal. He reads it aloud. It is eleven
words in an older dialect. Cael goes very still.

The annotation is the fourth verse. Compressed to eleven words, set in older
language, written by a later hand — but the same.

Someone knew. Someone who came before them, someone who understood what the
legend meant, added this note to the manuscript so that whoever found the
relics and failed would still have the instruction waiting for them in
their notes.

Oswin says: who wrote it.

Nobody knows. But Maren points out that the question of who wrote it is
separate from the question of whether it is useful. She asks if Cael can say
the prince's name. She asks if anyone knows the prince's name.

Oswin finds it in his journal. The king mentioned it during the palace meeting.
Cael did not write it down. Oswin did.

> **The method:** The three relics, having passed through the hands of the
> companions, have done their work. They showed each person what they were
> carrying. That process — moving through fear, through false story, through
> surrendered will — is what the legend was describing. The demon can take the
> objects back. It cannot take back what those objects showed.
>
> To break the possession: speak the prince's true name, using it as address
> rather than accusation, aimed past the demon at the person still inside.
> This cannot be done by someone who is afraid, or operating from a false
> story, or waiting for someone else's permission. It requires someone who
> has faced all three of those in themselves. The trials were not for the
> relics. They were for the person who would eventually stand in that throne
> room.

## Chapter 10 — The Resolution

They escape the cell. This is described without elaborate mechanics — Oswin,
who has spent several chapters noting every detail around him including the
guard rotation and the cell door's latch design, simply has the information
needed. He has been taking notes. Notes, it turns out, are useful.

The confrontation in the throne room does not look like a battle. The Demon
King does not charge. He asks what they think they are doing. Cael says: we
are going to speak to the prince.

The demon is contemptuous. It has ruled through fear, through control, through
the suppression of individual thought. It has no framework for understanding
how three people with no weapons, no relics, and no apparent power are going
to be a problem.

Cael says the prince's name. He says it plainly, not loudly, as if calling
across a room to someone who has been there the whole time and was only waiting
to be addressed.

The demon fights this. It is not a clean moment. The body on the throne
convulses, speaks in multiple voices, describes in detail everything the three
of them are afraid of, everything they have hidden from themselves. It tries
Maren's grief. It tries Cael's disbelief. It tries Oswin's trained passivity.
None of these work anymore, not entirely, though each of them has a harder
moment than they expected.

Maren, who does not have a weapon, stands next to Cael and says: keep going.

Cael keeps going. He says the prince's name a second time. A third. He
addresses the prince specifically — not the demon, not the body, not the
situation. He talks to the person who has been inside for years with no one
speaking to him.

The prince answers.

The resolution is slow and difficult and does not look like victory until it
is over. The demon is expelled. The prince is present, disoriented, exhausted,
and alive. He has not spoken in years. He does not know what year it is.
The first thing he says, quietly, is: *is it over.*

Maren says yes.

## Aftermath

The king is not destroyed. When the truth travels south — and it travels
quickly, because Oswin sends a full written account to every scriptorium in
the region before anyone can think to stop him — the king's position collapses.
He does not face a dramatic confrontation. He faces the administrative
dissolution of everything he built the lie to protect. His son is alive and
back, and the cost of that is everything else.

Whether this constitutes justice is not resolved in the text. It is left
to the reader, and to the kingdom.

The three companions leave the Osswall together and walk back south on the
same road they came north on. They are changed in ways they will need years
to fully understand. Oswin's journal is full. He is already planning a second
volume.

Cael, walking, hums *The Weight of Three* from beginning to end for the
first time without stopping.

He does not perform it. He just follows the song all the way through.

## Arc Function

The final arc resolves all planted threads. The king's question in chapter 1.
The lines Cael hummed. The annotation Oswin copied and found and copied again.
The warmth of the key. Each detail returns not as a twist but as a convergence —
the story was always pointing here, and the three companions were always the
right people to arrive at this point, not because they were chosen for their
strength, but because the trials found the specific weaknesses they each needed
to face.

The resolution is deliberately unglamorous. There is no magical climax. A
person says a name, carefully, to someone who needed to hear it. That is enough.
