# Chapter 3

<!-- trama:center:end -->

Three days north of Valdenmere, the road gave up its struggle against the incline and descended into a shallow, forested bowl. The trees pressed in close on either side of the track, their ancient, heavy canopies filtering the late afternoon sun into a bruised, underwater twilight. When the village of Drest finally appeared through the gloom, it looked entirely peaceful. It took perhaps five minutes of walking down its central track to identify exactly why it did not feel that way.

The houses were tidy, the thatch fresh, the yards swept. But the orderliness had a rigid, nervous edge to it. Though the sun had not yet touched the western ridge, shutters were already being pulled tight and latched from the inside. People moved between the communal well and their doorways with a rapid, purposeful gait, their heads down, eyes sliding past the three strangers with barely a flicker of acknowledgment. There was no lingering. There was no idle talk over fences. A community of two hundred people was putting itself away for the night with the synchronized efficiency of a fortress preparing for a siege.

At the center of the village stood an inn called the Low Hearth. Inside, the common room smelled of woodsmoke and old beer, reasonably warm and populated by a handful of locals finishing their evening meals in subdued voices. The innkeeper, a man named Keswick whose family had drawn ale here for three generations, possessed a demeanor of polite, frictionless efficiency. He answered Oswin’s questions about the road north without pausing in his work of wiping down the counter. He answered questions about the dense woodland surrounding the village without actually answering them, his responses as smooth and featureless as river stones.

Maren set her heavy pack down by the hearth and watched him. She knew what an innkeeper looked like when he was worried about unpaid tabs, rowdy drunks, or short provisions. This was different. Keswick was performing a kind of aggressive normalcy. He was working very hard to project a calm he did not feel.

"It is very dark under those trees," Oswin noted, adjusting the strap of his satchel. His ink-stained fingers tapped rhythmically against his leg. "I noticed a rather fascinating pillar near the treeline on our way in. Carved wood, hung with cloth, twice the height of a man. A votive structure, I assume? Local folklore is a particular interest of mine, you see."

Keswick did not stop wiping the bar, but the motion became slightly stiffer. "We keep the bounds," he said evenly. "It is an old custom. A pint of bitter for you, then?"

"Is it an offering?" Oswin pressed, oblivious to the sudden, heavy silence that had fallen over the three nearest tables. The detached enthusiasm of the scriptorium blinded him to the temperature of the room. "And if so, to what? A local spirit? A guardian?"

A farmer sitting at the bar stared into his cup, abruptly slid off his stool, and walked out the back door without a single word.

Before Oswin could ask another question, a man at a corner table pushed his chair back. The scrape of wood against the floorboards was loud in the sudden quiet. The man was older, his hair iron-grey, his clothes of a finer weave than the farmers'. He walked over to their table and sat down uninvited, placing his hands flat on the scarred wood.

"My name is Dreskin," the man said. His voice was a slow, practiced rumble, designed to carry across a room without sounding like a shout. "I am an elder of this village. If you have questions about the Deep, you should ask someone who knows the answers, rather than bothering a man who is trying to run a business."

Oswin blinked, instinctively reaching for the small notebook he kept in his breast pocket. "I would be honored, Elder Dreskin. I am a chronicler of—"

"Forty years ago," Dreskin interrupted, his tone carrying the smooth, worn tracks of a story told a thousand times, "something came out of those woods. We do not know what it was. We only know what it left behind."

Cael, who had been sitting quietly near the fire, turned his head.

Dreskin did not look at Cael. He looked at Oswin, recognizing the exact kind of listener who would write down his words and legitimize them. "It took animals first. Then it took people. It struck in the dark, without sound. For two years, this valley was an abattoir. It was not until the hunters banded together—led by a man of deep courage who became our first overseer, a responsibility my family holds to this day—that the thing was driven back into the Heart of the forest."

Oswin’s quill scratched frantically against the parchment. "Fascinating. A beast of the deep woods. And you believe it remains?"

"It remains," Dreskin said. The absolute certainty in his voice was heavy, suffocating. "It is cunning. It waits. The offerings we leave at the post satisfy its hunger, but only so long as we respect the boundaries. Only so long as the curfew is kept. Those who forget, those who grow careless or arrogant..." Dreskin paused, letting the silence expand perfectly to fill the space. "Four years ago, the Miller’s boy thought the stories were for children. He walked past the post after dusk. We found only his lantern, crushed in the roots of an oak."

The room was absolutely silent. The remaining patrons were looking down at their tables.

Cael watched Dreskin’s face, and then he looked at the patrons, and then he looked back at the fire. The bard felt a familiar, cold detachment settle over him. Cael made his living on stories. He knew their architecture. He knew how a narrative was built to evoke wonder, or sorrow, or courage.

And he knew exactly what a story sounded like when it was engineered as a cage.

Dreskin’s account was flawless. It was too flawless. The emotional weight was distributed precisely. The mention of his own family’s authority—slipped in as historical fact rather than present justification. The specific name of a victim, the Miller’s boy, used as a doubt-closer to shut down any skeptical inquiry. It was a narrative designed with no doors, no windows, and no exits. It did not invite questions; it flattened them under the weight of communal survival. Dreskin was not sharing history. He was maintaining a mechanism.

Cael said nothing. He simply leaned back, his face a pleasant, empty mask.

While Dreskin spoke, Maren’s attention had drifted. She was not interested in monsters. She was interested in the girl moving behind the bar.

Her name was Betta. She was perhaps fourteen, thin and sharp-elbowed, and she moved through the common room with the practiced invisibility of someone who had been working in adult spaces since she was small enough to hide under the tables. She gathered empty cups, wiped down spills, and fed logs into the hearth without ever quite meeting anyone’s eye.

But Maren, who had spent a decade watching thousands of people cross the threshold of her bread stall, knew how to read the set of a jaw and the slant of a shoulder. Betta did not look frightened. As Dreskin reached the grim conclusion of his tale, a couple of the farmers shuddered. Betta merely rolled her eyes, a microscopic twitch of irritation, before turning back to the washbasin.

She looked bored. It was the specific, bone-deep exhaustion of someone who had been afraid of the same thing her entire life and had finally run out of fear, leaving only a sour, nameless resentment in its place. She was a girl chained to a ghost, and she was tired of carrying the iron.

Maren watched her for a long moment, then quietly finished her cider.

By the time the sun fully set, the inn was empty of locals. The curfew was absolute. The heavy wooden bar was dropped across the main door, the thud echoing in the quiet room. Keswick retired to his quarters, leaving a single lamp burning low on the counter. Cael and Oswin went up to their shared room, Oswin still murmuring to himself as he reviewed his notes on the "Drest-thing," Cael following behind him with a silent, thoughtful shadow in his eyes.

Maren did not go upstairs. She picked up her travel pack and walked into the kitchen.

It was warm, smelling of rendered fat and stale yeast. Betta was standing at the large wooden sink, scrubbing a cast-iron pan with a handful of rough bristles. She jumped slightly when Maren entered, her shoulders tensing, but relaxed a fraction when she saw it was only the baker, not her father or an elder.

"Kitchen's closed," Betta said, her voice flat, trying for authority and missing by an inch.

"I don't need food," Maren said. She set her pack on the heavy preparation table and unbuckled the main flap. "I need an oven. If your father doesn't mind."

Betta frowned, scrubbing a stubborn patch of grease. "He's asleep. The fire's banked. You'd have to stoke it yourself."

"I know how to stoke a fire," Maren said. She pulled out a small, cloth-wrapped bundle of starter dough she kept alive on the road. "I've made my living by it."

Betta didn't object, which was permission enough. Maren went to work. She moved with an economy of motion that made the labor look effortless, waking the coals, feeding them kindling, and measuring out the flour she had bought two towns back. The rhythmic, repetitive work of kneading filled the silence in the kitchen.

For a long time, the only sounds were the scrape of bristles on iron and the soft, heavy thud of dough against wood.

Betta watched her covertly. Maren let her watch. People always talked eventually if you gave them space and a task to anchor them, but you couldn't rush them. You had to let them weigh you.

"That old man," Betta said suddenly, her voice barely louder than the crackle of the reviving fire. "Elder Dreskin. He talks a lot."

"He does," Maren agreed, not missing a beat in her kneading. Push, fold, turn.

Betta rinsed the pan and set it on the rack to dry. She leaned against the sink, wiping her wet hands on her apron. She was testing the water. Trying to see if Maren was the sort of traveler who traded local gossip for favor, or the sort who carried tales back to the authorities. Maren did not look up, projecting a calm, uninterested stability. It was the only way to pass the test.

"When I was eleven," Betta said, the words spilling out quickly now, as if she were afraid she would lose the nerve if she paused, "I got into a fight with the Miller’s boy. Not the one who died. His younger brother. He called me a coward because I wouldn't go near the back windows after dark. So I took a dare."

Maren paused her kneading, resting her flour-dusted hands on the table. She looked at the girl.

"I went to the treeline," Betta whispered, her eyes dark and intense in the low light. "Past curfew. I stood right by the offering post. I stood there for an hour. It was freezing, and I was terrified. I thought the Drest-thing was going to reach out of the dark and drag me into the Deep."

"And what happened?" Maren asked quietly.

Betta’s jaw tightened. "Nothing. Absolutely nothing. I heard an owl. I saw a badger. That was it. The offerings were just sitting there, rotting." She looked down at her hands. "I've watched the elders my whole life. The way they look when they talk about the beast. I sometimes think... I wonder if anyone has actually checked the forest in forty years. Or if they just like us being afraid."

Betta stopped, suddenly realizing how much she had said to a stranger. Her eyes went wide, a flash of genuine panic crossing her face. "I'd never say that to anyone who would tell my father. Or Dreskin. They'd say I was inviting a curse. They'd..."

"Betta," Maren said.

The girl stopped breathing.

Maren held her gaze. Her voice was perfectly level, stripped of any condescension or false comfort. "You're asking the right questions."

Betta exhaled a shaky breath. Some of the tight, defensive posture drained out of her shoulders. She didn't smile, but the exhaustion in her eyes lifted just a fraction, replaced by the quiet vindication of being seen.

Maren turned back to her dough. "Dry the rest of those bowls, and then go to bed. I'll watch the fire."

"Yes, ma'am," Betta said softly.

When the last dish was dried and put away, Betta gave Maren a brief, solemn nod and slipped out of the kitchen. Maren stayed with the bread. She shaped the loaves, let them proof in the warmth of the hearth, and baked them until the crusts were dark and hollow-sounding when tapped. By the time she finished, it was well past midnight. She wrapped the fresh bread in clean cloth, banked the fire properly so Keswick wouldn't have anything to complain about in the morning, and picked up her pack.

The hallway upstairs was dark and entirely silent, save for the faint groaning of the inn’s timbers settling in the night wind.

As Maren passed the door to the room Cael and Oswin were sharing, she caught the faint murmur of voices. She paused, her hand lightly resting on the wall.

It was Cael speaking. His voice lacked the melodic, projected resonance he used when performing; it was quiet, analytical, and cold.

"...it’s the architecture of it, Oswin," Cael was saying. "You're looking at the details of the monster. You need to look at the shape of the story. Notice how it serves them. The monster never attacks the village directly—it only punishes those who step out of line, who break curfew, who question the boundaries. It's too convenient. It’s not a recounting of a tragedy. It’s a mechanism. It’s a story designed entirely to keep people from leaving."

There was a pause, and then the scratch of Oswin’s quill. "So you believe the creature... what, migrated? Died?"

"I don't know what happened forty years ago," Cael replied softly. "But I know what is happening today. They are managing this town with a ghost."

Maren did not stay to listen to the rest. She did not need to. She already understood the shape of things here. She moved down the hall to her own small room, opened the door, and set her pack on the floor.

She undressed in the dark and got into the narrow bed. Outside her window, the village of Drest kept its absolute, breathless curfew. At the edge of the trees, the carved wooden post stood in the dark, hung with cloth and laden with food, doing exactly what it had always done.

Maren closed her eyes, and went to sleep.
