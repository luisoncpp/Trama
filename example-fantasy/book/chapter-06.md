# Chapter 6

<!-- trama:center:end -->

The morning after the festival always carries a specific kind of exhaustion. The square in Solen's Crossing, which only hours before had been a chaotic tangle of cheering crowds, spilled ale, and the theatrical clash of prop swords, now lay quiet and gray under the early light. The banners commemorating Aldric the Warden hung limp in the still air. A few municipal sweepers worked methodical arcs across the cobblestones, gathering discarded pamphlets and crushed festival garlands into unceremonious piles.

Cael, Maren, and Oswin walked through the square without speaking. Cael looked at the wooden stage where the theatrical company had performed the founding myth. In the cold light of morning, stripped of its audience and its momentum, it was just a raised platform of scuffed pine boards.

They reached the Archive of Solen shortly after the heavy bronze doors were unlocked for the day. Inside, the air was cool and smelled faintly of binding glue and old dust. Pell, the archivist who had given them such an exhaustively helpful tour the previous afternoon, was already occupied at the front desk, patiently explaining the cataloging system to a visiting scholar who seemed determined not to understand it.

They moved past the main reading tables, deeper into the stacks.

"The structural annex," Oswin whispered, unfolding his copy of the official map. He pointed to a blank square on the northwest corner of the schematic. "Pell said it was sealed due to subsidence. But if you walk the exterior of the building, the stone footing extends a full thirty feet further west than the interior walls suggest. The slope of the riverbank accommodates a lower level. The discrepancy is small if you aren't looking for it, but the geometry is consistent."

"You measured the building from the outside?" Maren asked, her voice kept carefully low.

"I paced it," Oswin corrected, adjusting his satchel. "The map is a fiction. Or, at least, an incomplete truth."

They found the access point with surprisingly little effort. At the back of the ground-floor stacks, behind a heavy oak shelving unit devoted to three-hundred-year-old taxation decrees, there was a seam in the panelling. The shelf itself had not been moved in years, judging by the unbroken layer of dust settled around its base, but there was a narrow gap between its edge and the stone wall. Behind the gap, a door sat flush with the masonry.

It was not locked. It was not blocked by rubble. There was no sign of structural damage.

Cael glanced back toward the front of the Archive. The murmur of Pell's voice drifted through the quiet aisles. Cael pushed the door inward. The hinges made a faint, papery sigh, but did not squeal.

They stepped through and pulled the door shut behind them.

The lower level was a long, dry corridor, stretching much farther than the map's supposed annex would have allowed. The air down here was different—staler, but lacking the damp rot that usually plagued riverside basements. Someone had gone to considerable expense to ensure the moisture from the Solen river did not penetrate the stone.

Shelves ran from floor to ceiling on both sides of the corridor. The documents were meticulously organized. Thick ledgers bound in hardened leather, bundles of parchment tied with faded ribbon, and wooden scroll-cases sat in precise rows. Small brass plaques nailed to the edge of each shelf marked the years.

Cael ran a finger along the edge of a shelf. It came away with a fine layer of dust, but not a thick one. "Someone maintains this room," he observed quietly. "Not often. But it hasn't been abandoned."

Oswin was already moving down the row. He stopped at a section marked with a date three centuries past—the era of the town's founding. He set his satchel on the floor, took out a ledger, and opened it.

He did not speak again for twenty minutes.

The silence in the lower level was absolute, heavy with the accumulated weight of thousands of unread pages. Cael walked a few paces down the aisle, then slid down the wall until he was sitting on the stone floor, his back resting against the edge of a shelf. He pulled his knees up and watched the dust motes drift in the faint light filtering from the small ventilation grates near the ceiling.

Maren did not sit. She stood beside Oswin for a moment, looking over his shoulder at the ledgers. She read a few pages of a commercial contract, traced a line of numbers with her eyes, and then flipped forward to a summary of shipping tolls. Within three minutes, the subtle tightening around her eyes indicated she understood exactly what she was looking at. She closed the ledger she was holding, placed it back on the shelf, and leaned against the wall to wait for the scribe to catch up.

Oswin read with a consuming, breathless intensity. He cross-referenced ledgers with loose letters. He checked dates against municipal seals. He traced the handwriting of different clerks. Finally, he closed a heavy, brass-clasped book. The sound of it shutting was a dull thud in the quiet room.

When he spoke, his voice was very quiet.

"The founding account," Oswin said, staring at the closed book, "is not accurate."

Cael looked up from the floor. Maren simply crossed her arms. "Go on," she said.

"There was no army," Oswin said, his tone perfectly flat, the way it always got when documentary evidence was unambiguous. "The 'foreign invaders' from the legend. There was no army at all. There was a protracted legal and territorial dispute between two prominent merchant families over bridge rights and access to the river trade. It destabilized the region's economy for two decades."

He gestured to the rows of ledgers. "Aldric was not a warrior. He was a mediator. An arbiter hired jointly by both families to resolve the dispute. He was extremely competent, and genuinely useful to the region, but he never drove off a horde at swordpoint. The 'peace treaty' he forged was a commercial contract establishing a shared tariff structure."

"And the bridge?" Cael asked, his voice rough.

"The bridge was built by a collective of forty-three stone-workers over three years," Oswin replied, his gaze tracking the spines of the ledgers. "Most of them were contracted from outside the town. None of their names are on the monument upstairs. The merchant families who benefited from the tariff arrangement commissioned the Aldric legend a generation later. It erased any competing claims on the bridge, and it gave the town a unified, heroic identity. It was simply better for business than the truth."

While Oswin had been speaking, Cael had pushed himself up from the floor and begun walking toward the far end of the corridor. The air grew slightly cooler here, the shelves holding older, more fragile boxes.

He found the relic without looking for it.

It sat on a shelf at eye height, resting in a cloth wrapping that had mostly rotted away, leaving gray, threadbare lacework over something dark and solid.

Cael reached out and picked it up. It was heavier than he expected.

He pulled the remnants of the cloth away. It was a disc of polished black glass, roughly the size of a dessert plate, edged in tarnished silver. The Still Mirror.

He turned it over in his hands. The glass was perfectly smooth, flawless despite sitting in the dark for three hundred years. He raised it and looked into its surface.

He did not see his own face.

The black glass did not reflect the person holding it. Instead, it showed him what was behind him. In the dark surface of the mirror, he saw the long corridor of the lower level. He saw the shelves. He saw the ledgers, the bundles of parchment, the commercial contracts, the mundane, unheroic truth of the merchant families and the forty-three nameless stone-workers. He saw every piece of evidence that had been sitting here undisturbed while the story above them accumulated festivals, statues, and cheering crowds.

He stood there for a long moment, gripping the silver edge of the glass.

Maren walked up beside him. Her footsteps were nearly silent on the stone. She looked at the object in his hands, then up at his face.

"What do you see?" she asked.

Cael did not lower the mirror. He stared into the reflection of the archive.

"What was already here," he said.

It was not the answer she had asked for, but she recognized the tone of his voice, the quiet, hollow edge of it, and she accepted it. She did not press him.

What Cael was not saying—what he could not bring himself to say aloud—was that he was part of the machinery of the lie.

He had performed the Aldric legend. He had stood in a crowded tavern two towns over, the firelight catching his face, and he had sung the battle of the bridge. He had described the flash of Aldric's sword, the terror of the invading army, the blood on the stones. He had shaped the narrative, polished the rough edges of the pacing, made the dialogue sharper and the climax more thrilling. He had made the story better for his audience, and they had paid him well for it.

He had always told himself that this was simply how stories worked. It was performance. It was harmless, because everyone in the room knew it was a show. A bard's job was not to be a historian; a bard's job was to make people feel something.

But standing in this hidden room, surrounded by the unperformed version of the story he had sold for coin, that comfortable justification collapsed. The stories he performed did not stay in the tavern halls. They seeped out into the cobblestones. They became the pamphlet the actors used. They became what the children were taught. They became the unquestioned reality of a town that built statues to a fiction and buried the men who actually built their bridge.

Stories were not harmless. What people believed shaped what they protected, and what they chose to destroy. He had spent his life treating truth as a raw material, something to be bent and discarded if it didn't serve the rhythm of a song.

He had never wondered if there was a room below the archive, because the room below the archive was not useful to the performance.

Slowly, carefully, Cael wrapped the black glass in the remnants of its cloth. He placed it securely in his pack, pulling the leather straps tight. He did not look at Maren or Oswin as he turned toward the door.

"We have what we came for," Cael said. "Let's go."

They made their way back down the corridor. As they reached the door, Oswin hesitated. He looked back at the shelves of ledgers, his hand resting on the iron latch.

"Should we tell anyone?" Oswin asked. His brow was furrowed in genuine distress. The scribe in him was agonizing over the suppression of the historical record. "The evidence is all here. The truth is fully documented. Surely someone should do something with this information."

Maren paused beside him. She looked at Oswin, not with pity, but with a weary, pragmatic clarity.

"Tell who, Oswin?" she asked quietly. "And what do you expect them to do with it?"

Oswin opened his mouth, then closed it. He looked up toward the ceiling, in the direction of the festival square, the statues, the town guard, the prosperous merchants who profited from the bridge's legend. He thought about this.

Cael thought about it for longer, and far less productively, because he was no longer just thinking about the town of Solen's Crossing. He was thinking about every town he had ever visited, and every song he had ever sung.

Before they left the lower level, as Maren and Cael slipped through the door back into the dusty gap behind the bookshelf, Oswin lingered for a final moment. He had stopped near a shelf containing property dispute records from the earliest days of the settlement.

His eyes, trained by years of deciphering ruined texts, caught an anomaly.

Sticking out slightly from between two thick bundles of commercial decrees was a single sheet of paper. It did not belong there. The paper itself was different—vellum, not parchment, brittle with a specific kind of age that did not match the supple ledgers around it.

Oswin slid the page out.

The handwriting was entirely different from the clerks' neat script. It was cramped, hurried, and written in an ink that had faded to a rust-brown. The text was a short verse in a dialect that predated the founding of Solen's Crossing by at least a century.

Oswin's breath hitched. He recognized it immediately.

It was the marginal annotation from the original manuscript of the legend. The exact same lines. He had copied this annotation four times during his years at the scriptorium. He knew the shape of every letter, even though he had never fully parsed its meaning.

He stared at the page. Why was it here? Why was a fragment of an ancient, sacred legend tucked deliberately between two three-hundred-year-old property records in a commercial archive hidden beneath a river town? It had not been misplaced. It had been planted. Someone had put it here, specifically among these records.

Oswin did not stop to analyze it further. The mystery was too vast, too incongruous with the room he was standing in. Instead, he did what he always did when faced with information he could not comprehend: he recorded it.

He pulled his journal from his satchel, balanced it against the shelf, and quickly, perfectly, copied the verse. When he was finished, he drew a neat, square box around the text. It was the second box in his journal, containing the exact same content as the first.

He slipped the original vellum page back between the property records, exactly where he had found it.

On his way back upstairs, stepping through the concealed door and into the bright, dusty light of the main archive, he almost opened his mouth to mention it to the others. He looked at Cael's tense shoulders, and Maren's guarded expression, and he closed his mouth again.

He was not yet ready to explain what he did not understand.
