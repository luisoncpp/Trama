# Chapter 2 — The Road

<!-- trama:center:end -->

The north road out of Valdenmere was never designed for grand departures or heroic beginnings. It was a trade artery, carved into the hard earth by generations of iron-rimmed wheels, heavy hooves, and the exhausted boots of merchants. Built for utility rather than spectacle, it offered no scenic overlooks, no dramatic vistas to inspire a traveler. Two hours past the city gates, the soaring stone architecture of the capital had long since dissolved into flat, unremarkable plains of yellowing grass. The sky above was a sheet of indifferent, bruised grey.

Cael walked steadily, though his boots were entirely wrong for the task. He carried little more than his stringed instrument—worn smooth at the neck from years of playing—strapped tightly across his back, and a satchel of provisions that felt painfully light against his ribs. He did not own a horse. The king’s generous purse was currently tucked deep into the inner lining of his coat, pressed against his chest, but spending it within sight of the capital felt like an excellent way to invite a robbery. So, he kept his head down and his pace even, listening to the dry wind scrape across the plains. He was on a quest, theoretically. The fate of the realm supposedly rested on his shoulders. Mostly, his feet hurt, and he was thirsty.

Then a cart caught up to him, and the journey truly began.

It did not arrive with a dramatic clatter or a shout. The cart approached with a rhythmic, well-maintained creak, the massive draft horse pulling it moving at a slow, unimpeachable plod that ate up the miles without seeming to hurry. Cael stepped to the side of the road to let it pass.

It did not pass. The horse drew level with him, blowing hot air from its nostrils, and the driver pulled back gently on the reins.

The woman on the bench was in her late thirties. Her face was weathered by the heat of fires and outdoor labor, her hands wrapped firmly around the leather straps with a casual, ingrained competence. She wore a heavy canvas apron over her dress, dusted faintly with white powder. She did not immediately speak.

For exactly four seconds, her eyes swept over Cael. It was an intensely practical examination. She took in the lute, the impractical boots, the lack of a mount, and the quiet, wary set of his shoulders. She assessed him the way a master carpenter assesses a slightly warped plank—gauging whether it would hold weight or snap under pressure. She did not seem particularly impressed, but she did not seem disgusted, either.

"The road gets worse past the river crossing," she said. Her voice was flat, carrying the firm cadence of someone who did not repeat herself.

"I've heard that," Cael replied cautiously, keeping his distance.

"There are deep ruts. Mud, if it rains. The wheels get stuck, and I can't lift the back axle out of a trench by myself." She gestured with a minute tilt of her head toward the empty space beside her on the bench. "I have room. You have a back. You ride, and when the cart gets stuck, you push."

Cael looked past her, analyzing the cart. It was a masterpiece of spatial economy. The payload was loaded with considerable precision. He spotted a disassembled brick oven, strapped down in two heavy sections to balance the weight over the axle. Behind it were two massive sacks of flour wrapped tightly in waxed canvas to keep out the damp, a toolkit bound in a thick leather roll, and a wooden crate of provisions organized meticulously by type and shelf life. Every item had a purpose. Nothing rattled. None of it suggested a casual traveler out for a leisurely trip.

"That seems like a fair trade," Cael said.

"It is," she said. "I'm Maren. Don't play that instrument while I'm trying to listen to the road."

"Cael. And I won't."

He climbed up. The wooden bench was hard and lacked a cushion, but it was infinitely better than walking. Maren clicked her tongue, a sharp, practiced sound, and the heavy draft horse resumed its steady pace.

They did not discuss the quest. They did not speak of the king, or the prophecy, or the demon occupying the northern fortress. Maren, of course, knew who he was. The king's summons had been public enough, and Cael's abrupt, escorted disappearance from the Gilded Post tavern had been noted in certain circles. She knew he was walking north on a fool's errand. He knew, intuitively, that she was going north for a different kind of survival—the silent, heavy grief radiating from the way she kept her eyes fixed firmly on the horizon told him she was leaving something behind.

But she did not ask about his destiny, and he did not pry into her grief. She was going north for her own reasons. He was going north for his. The cart had room, and the road was dangerous for a solitary traveler. That was the entirety of the transaction.

By midday, the dull monotony of the journey was broken by the sound of rapid, uneven footsteps approaching from behind.

Cael turned on the bench, looking over the waxed canvas goods. A young man was jogging toward them. He was perhaps nineteen, sweating profusely in the cool air, and burdened by a satchel that was dramatically overstuffed. It bounced awkwardly against his hip with every step, threatening to spill its contents. The frantic swinging of the bag suggested its owner had never successfully prioritized when packing for a trip in his entire life.

The young man reached the cart and jogged alongside it for a few agonizing paces, gasping for air and clutching his side, before Maren let out a long, long sigh and brought the horse to a halt.

"Are you—" the young man panted, bracing a hand on his knees, his face flushed a violent shade of red. "Are you Cael? The bard?"

Cael exchanged a brief, dry glance with Maren. "Usually."

The young man stood up, attempting to smooth out his robes, which were the rough, undyed wool of a scriptorium initiate. He reached into his disorganized satchel, fumbling past what looked like three separate ink pots, pulled out a scroll bound in a heavy wax seal, and presented his credentials. He did this with the rigid formality of someone who had rehearsed this exact introduction in front of a mirror for three hours and was profoundly relieved to finally be using it.

"I am Oswin," he declared, though his voice cracked slightly on the second syllable, undermining the gravitas. "Apprentice of the fourth circle at the Royal Scriptorium. I have been dispatched by Abbot Helmi to serve as the official chronicler of this expedition. I am to observe, document, and return with a firsthand, historically accurate account of the trials and the reclamation of the relics."

He paused, taking a desperate, rattling breath. "I apologize for my delay. I took a wrong turn at the tanner's district. Has... has the quest officially begun? Have I missed anything?"

Maren looked at the boy. She looked at his absurdly large satchel, at his sweat-drenched brow, and then back at the empty, dusty road ahead of them.

"It has not," she said.

"I'm not sure it ever started," Cael added gently.

Oswin blinked. Then, with sudden frantic energy, he produced a leather-bound travel journal and a charcoal stick from his robes. He opened the book, wrote down both of their responses with furious speed, and checked the angle of the sun. "Noted. Timestamped. Thank you."

Maren did not offer Oswin a seat on the bench—there was no room, and the draft horse was pulling enough weight—but she did allow him to stow his massive satchel in a narrow gap between the flour sacks. Unburdened, Oswin managed to keep pace with the cart on foot without collapsing from exhaustion.

The rest of the day’s travel settled into a rhythm. The three of them established, without ever discussing or agreeing to it, a working pattern. Maren handled the practical logistics of the road, steering the horse around potholes, identifying the firmest ground, and dictating when they stopped to rest the animal. Oswin recorded things. He walked alongside the cart, sometimes murmuring under his breath as he drafted sentences in his head, occasionally writing in his journal while walking—a skill that resulted in a few minor stumbles over rocks but yielded surprisingly legible handwriting.

Cael watched them both. He said very little, which meant, by the time the sun began to dip toward the horizon, he had already catalogued a great deal about his new companions. He noted that Maren checked the tension on the oven straps every time the cart hit a bump, a habit born of relying solely on one's own equipment and trusting no one else to secure it. He noticed that Oswin apologized to the horse whenever he accidentally brushed against its flank. Cael shared none of these observations. He simply sat, listened to the creak of the wheels, and let the miles pass.

By late afternoon, the initial awkwardness of strangers thrust together had worn down into a comfortable enough silence. No one felt the desperate need to fill the quiet with chatter, which was, in Cael's experience, progress of a sort.

Dusk brought them to a crossroads inn. It was not a place of comfort, charm, or safety; it was a low, timber-framed building that existed purely because travelers needed a place to sleep before the territory turned wild and truly dangerous.

Inside, the common room was dark, smoky, and smelled of roasted root vegetables, damp wool, and old ale. The hearth fire crackled weakly in the corner, fighting a losing battle against the damp. A few rough-looking travelers sat at the tables, but a single, flat glare from Maren kept them from looking too closely at Cael's lute or the heavy canvas bags they brought inside. She negotiated their lodging with the innkeeper, securing three pallets in a shared room and a pot of stew for a price that made the innkeeper look slightly bruised. She paid with a combination of copper coin and half a loaf of crusty bread she had baked before leaving the city.

They sat at a scarred wooden table near the fire. The stew was mostly cabbage and broth, but it was hot, and it settled the dust in their throats.

Oswin, who had clearly been waiting to have this conversation since he caught up to the cart at noon, finally put down his wooden spoon. He opened his journal to a fresh page, carefully uncorked a small ink pot, and cleared his throat.

"Given that we are approaching the northern territories," Oswin began, his tone shifting into the pedantic cadence of a scriptorium lecturer, "I thought it prudent to review the known history of the three relics, as detailed in the original manuscript."

Maren paused, her spoon halfway to her mouth. Cael did not look up from his bowl.

"The manuscript," Oswin continued, taking their silence as encouragement, "is quite clear on the geography and the metaphysical requirements. The relics are housed in three locations: the Hollow of Drest, the Archive of Solen, and the Monastery of the Bound Hand. Each is guarded by a trial of character. The text states that the first trial tests the purity of one's courage, the second the sharpness of one's mind, and the third the endurance of one's spirit. Only by passing these internal trials can the relics be claimed, and only with the relics can the Demon King be unmade."

He finished with a satisfied nod, resting his hands on the table. He had the text nearly memorized. He was precise, thorough, and clearly pleased to finally be putting his years of meticulous study to practical use.

Maren chewed a piece of cabbage slowly. She swallowed. She looked at Oswin as if he were a particularly complicated puzzle she didn't have the time to solve.

"Which version are you quoting?" she asked.

Oswin blinked, his quill hovering over the page. "I'm sorry?"

"Which version of the story?" Maren asked, resting her elbows on the sticky table. "I heard a completely different account from a traveling storyteller at the market in Oakhaven a few years back. He said the relics were in a different order. And the trials weren't about 'purity' or 'mind.' They were external. Physical traps. Spikes, poisoned arrows, crushing walls, that sort of thing. And the ending involved considerably more blood than whatever it is you're describing."

Oswin's expression tightened with scholarly offense. He looked genuinely wounded by the suggestion. "With respect, madam, there is only one original manuscript. It is housed in the royal archives under lock and key. What you heard was likely a peasant corruption, embellished for dramatic effect at a market stall to earn more coin."

"Maybe," Maren said smoothly, entirely unbothered by his tone. "But that storyteller was very confident."

"Confidence does not equate to historical accuracy," Oswin insisted, his voice rising a fraction of an octave, drawing a glance from the next table. "The scriptorium's copies are exact. We do not deviate from the source. The trials are internal. The relics are real."

Cael scraped the bottom of his bowl. He set his spoon down and, without looking up at either of them, spoke into the quiet tension.

"There are at least four distinct versions in circulation," Cael said, his voice mild, resonant, and carrying the natural projection of a performer, even when he spoke softly. "The Oakhaven variant she mentioned is popular in the east. But the oldest one I know—the one I learned as a song when I was a boy—doesn't include the relics at all. It ends differently."

Oswin turned to him, horrified. "Doesn't include the relics? But the relics are the entire point of the prophecy! Without them, there is no mechanism for victory!"

"Not in that song," Cael said. He leaned back in his chair, his eyes fixed on the dull, pulsing embers in the hearth. "It's an awkward piece of music. The meter is strange. It's too strange to hold an audience, really. I rarely perform it."

He didn't name the song. He didn't say it was called _The Weight of Three_. But in the smoky, ale-soaked air of the inn, he let out a slow breath and recited two lines from memory:

_"The iron crown upon the hollow shore,_ _Requires no lock, but opens every door."_

The words hung in the air. They sounded pleasant enough, carrying the familiar, slightly hypnotic rhythm of old verse. But they were also somewhat imprecise, vague enough to mean everything and nothing. To anyone else in the room, there was nothing particularly special about them at all.

Oswin’s quill scratched against the paper. He wrote the two lines down immediately, his brow furrowed in deep, troubled concentration. Then, a memory surfaced—a fragment from the margins of the master manuscript he had copied at the scriptorium. It was an eleven-word annotation in an older dialect that he had never fully understood, but its cadence suddenly mirrored the strange meter of Cael's song perfectly.

He quickly scribbled the eleven words beneath Cael's couplet: _Speak the true name past the shadow, wake the buried son._

He didn't know why, but seeing them together made his stomach tighten. They didn't fit the rigid structure of the prose he had memorized, yet they belonged together in some unseen puzzle. Driven by an instinct he couldn't name, Oswin took his quill and carefully drew a small, dark box around the eleven-word annotation.

In the margin next to the box, he added a note in his tiny, flawless script: _This annotation from the manuscript margin matches the meter of the bard's song. Not sure why this bothers me._

Nobody said anything for a long moment.

The fire popped loudly, sending a shower of orange sparks up the soot-stained chimney. Outside, the wind howled around the eaves, and the north road stretched out into the dark, quiet and indifferent.

Three people who had known each other for approximately ten hours sat around a scarred table, burdened by a small, unexplained discrepancy between one version of a legend and another. They didn't realize it yet, but the trap had already closed around them. The story was already less stable than any of them had assumed.

The moment passed. Maren stood up, pushing her chair back with a loud scrape against the floorboards.

"I'm going to sleep," she announced, picking up her pack. "We leave at first light. The cart doesn't wait."

Oswin carefully blew on his journal to dry the ink, closed it with a soft thud, and stood as well. Cael remained seated for a moment longer, watching the fire die down to a dim, glowing ash, before finally pushing himself up from the table.

They went to their respective pallets in the shared room. They were not yet a team. They were three people going the exact same direction, who had agreed, so far, not to make things harder for each other.

It was a reasonable beginning.
