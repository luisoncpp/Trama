# Chapter 4 — The Forest

The morning after the elder delivered his account, Cael laid a rough map on the scarred wooden table of the Low Hearth’s common room. It was drawn in charcoal on a scrap of butcher’s paper, the lines faint and hesitant, mapping the shape of the Hollow of Drest not as a physical landscape, but as a topography of fear.

The common room was quiet. A few farmers sat near the hearth, nursing watered ale and speaking in the low, guarded murmurs that seemed to define the village’s morning routine. Cael kept his voice beneath the ambient noise, leaning in so that Oswin could see the charcoal marks.

"Here," Cael said, tapping a smudge that represented the village square. He drew his finger upward, toward a dense grouping of dark lines. "This is north. The direction the hunters supposedly drove the creature forty years ago. But when Dreskin spoke last night, the territory he warned against most fiercely wasn’t north."

Oswin leaned closer, his eyes narrowing as he adjusted his spectacles. He smelled faintly of ink and the damp wool of his cloak. "He specifically mentioned the Deep," Oswin said, his voice carrying the exact cadence of a scribe recalling a text verbatim. "The eastern expanse. He said the trees there were oldest, the shadows thickest. He claimed the most recent disappearances occurred along the eastern ridge."

"Exactly," Cael said softly. "East. Away from the road. Farthest from the one route outsiders could use to enter or leave the valley."

Oswin frowned, tracing the logic. "If a predatory animal were hunting near the village, it would approach from the nearest dense cover, not the farthest. The eastern ridge is nearly three miles from the nearest pasture. It’s an inefficient hunting ground."

"It’s not just inefficient," Cael said. "It’s narrative misdirection. You point the fear at the place nobody has any reason to walk toward. If you tell them the danger is in the north, someone might accidentally stumble into it looking for stray sheep or firewood. But the east? It’s deep woods. Rough terrain. There is nothing there but trees. The only reason anyone would ever go there is to look for the creature." Cael sat back, looking at the charcoal lines. "The fear has been pointed the wrong way."

Oswin made a precise, tiny note in his journal, his quill scratching softly against the parchment. "How sure are you?"

Cael looked at the map. He thought of the ballads he had sung in a hundred different taverns, the stories meant to keep children in their beds, the tales designed to make a lord look heroic and his enemies look monstrous. A false story always had a specific geometry to it. It was too neat. It answered the questions it wanted you to ask, and obscured the ones it didn't.

"I am not sure enough to bet my life on it without a second thought," Cael said honestly. "But I am sure enough to walk."

Maren arrived at the table midway through his sentence, carrying a pitcher of water and three wooden cups. She set them down with the practiced efficiency of a woman who had spent half her life managing a crowded counter. She did not ask for permission to join the conversation. She simply took her seat, poured the water, and looked at the charcoal map.

"What are we walking toward?" she asked.

Cael summarized the geographic problem. He did not embellish it. He laid out the inconsistencies in the elder's story, the improbable hunting patterns, the convenient isolation of the eastern woods.

Maren listened in silence, her gaze moving from Cael to Oswin, and finally to the dark smudge on the paper that represented the eastern Deep. She took a slow sip of water. The practicality in her eyes was cold and absolute.

"Tonight, then," she said.

The wait was worse than the decision.

The chapter of the day stretched out, long and brittle. The group remained at the inn, burdened by the silent knowledge of what they intended to do when the sun went down. Drest went about its business, entirely unaware of the fracture forming in its carefully maintained reality.

Oswin spent the afternoon sitting on a bench outside the inn, ostensibly reading a heavy tome of regional history, but actually cataloging the offering post at the edge of the treeline. He observed it with the detached precision of a scholar cataloging a ruin. He noted the height of the carved wooden pillar, the exact placement of the iron hooks, the woven baskets of grain, the faded ribbons fluttering in the stagnant breeze. He timed how often villagers glanced toward the post as they went about their chores. It was a rhythmic, subconscious checking, like touching a pocket to ensure a coin was still there.

Inside the common room, Cael listened. He was good at listening. It was the other half of a bard's trade, the part they rarely paid you for. He sat by the fire, tuning his lute, letting the fragmented conversations of the village wash over him.

He learned, from two separate, hushed conversations between older women mending nets by the window, that no one had actually been taken by the Drest-thing in at least eight years. The most recent story, spoken of in hushed, terrified tones, involved a farmhand named Elian who had "gone too close" to the eastern ridge and was found wandering the next morning, confused and exhausted.

_Confused,_ Cael thought, plucking a discordant string. _Not mauled. Not hunted. Confused. Which could mean he drank too much cider and got lost in the dark._

The narrative was fraying the closer he looked.

Maren did not listen to stories, and she did not catalog posts. She went to the inn's kitchen. She spoke to the innkeeper's wife, offered a few copper coins, and spent the latter half of the afternoon baking. She used the flour she carried in her own cart, kneading the dough with steady, rhythmic violence.

She did not explain why she was baking. Cael watched her from the doorway for a moment, observing the sharp line of her jaw, the flour dusting her forearms, the sheer force she applied to the dough. She was grounding herself in the physical world, tying her mind to the undeniable reality of yeast, water, heat, and time. It was a defense mechanism masquerading as pragmatism, though Cael knew better than to say so aloud. She baked two small, dense loaves, wrapped them in cloth, and placed them in her pack.

When dusk finally fell, the village of Drest shuttered itself. It was not a gradual closing, but a sudden, synchronized retreat. Doors were barred. Shutters were latched. The silence that descended on the valley was not the peaceful quiet of evening, but the held breath of a trapped animal.

There was no moon to speak of.the

The three of them slipped out the back door of the Low Hearth. They crossed the dark expanse of the village square, moving quietly past the shuttered windows. They reached the eastern treeline, passing the wooden offering post where the baskets of grain and strips of cloth hung like a surrender flag in the windless night.

They stepped into the Deep.

The forest was not dangerous.

That was the truth of the trial, though knowing the truth did little to stop the body's reaction to the dark. There were no monsters lurking in the underbrush. There was no ancient, malicious intelligence tracking their movements. There was only the wood: old trees, tangled undergrowth, the damp smell of decaying leaves, and the sudden, startling rustle of small animals disturbed by their passage.

But the fear was a physical presence. It was not born from the forest, but from the decades of careful narrative planted in their minds. The elder's voice echoed in the dark, describing the way the creature moved without sound, the way it could mimic a human cry, the way the shadows seemed to lengthen just before it struck. Every snapping twig sounded like a bone breaking. Every shift in the wind felt like a breath against the back of the neck. It was a terror entirely constructed by other people, yet it worked regardless.

Oswin began to mutter. At first, Cael thought the scribe was praying, but as they pushed deeper into the suffocating gloom, the words became clear.

"_Quercus robur_, the common oak, characterized by its lobed leaves and deep taproot. _Fagus sylvatica_, the beech, smooth bark, shade-tolerant canopy..."

Oswin was reciting botanical facts under his breath. He was not doing it to inform them—he knew these facts perfectly well—but because his voice, moving through the rigid, logical structure of familiar knowledge, gave his mind something to hold onto. It was a tether to the rational world in a place built of irrational terror.

Cael found his own tether. He began to hum.

He didn't choose a heroic ballad or a song of battle. He hummed fragments of old, meandering folk tunes. He did not do it to comfort himself. He did it because a song has a structure, a progression of chords that must follow one another. Moving from verse to verse gave his mind a path to walk when his body wanted to freeze. It gave him something to follow when the next shadow shifted in the corner of his eye and his lungs tightened with panic.

Maren did not recite facts, and she did not hum. She walked ahead of both of them.

She held no lantern, relying only on the faint, ambient starlight filtering through the dense canopy to find the path. Her pace was steady, unyielding. She did not look over her shoulder. She did not flinch when an owl took flight from a nearby branch with a sudden clatter of wings. She simply moved forward, a singular point of practical momentum cutting through the constructed nightmare of the Deep.

They walked for an hour, the terrain growing steeper, the trees thicker, until the dense brush suddenly gave way to a small, natural clearing.

It was exactly where the geography of the fear had pointed.

At the center of the clearing, surrounded by ancient, towering oaks that seemed to bend away from it, sat a flat-topped rock formation. It rose about knee-high from the forest floor, its surface smooth and undisturbed. The forest had grown up around it without ever quite touching it.

And there, resting in the center of the outcropping, was the Shard of Stone.

It was entirely unremarkable in appearance. It was pale grey, roughly the size of a fist, and sat on the rock as if it had simply been dropped there and forgotten. It did not glow. It did not hum with ancient power. It was just a rock.

Maren reached the outcropping first.

She stopped, crouching beside the stone. The silence in the clearing was absolute, broken only by Oswin’s breathless muttering tapering off into nothing, and the soft, abrupt end of Cael’s humming.

Maren looked at the stone for a long moment. She reached out and placed her hand on it.

The moment her fingers closed around the rough grey surface, she understood its weight. It was not merely physical weight, though the stone was impossibly heavy for its size, dragging at her wrist like a block of solid lead. It was a correspondence. The stone was exactly as heavy as the things she had been carrying for two years.

She felt the crushing, suffocating weight of the empty house after her husband, Tomas, died. The terrifying silence of a morning without his voice. The panic of watching the flour reserves dwindle at the stall. The hollow, aching fear of packing her life into a single cart and arriving in a new town where no one knew her name, where she was nobody.

She had been carrying all of it. She had called it practicality. She had called it good sense. She had told herself that staying busy, moving forward, filling the unbearable silence with the physical labor of kneading dough and lighting fires was simply what a person had to do to survive. She had used forward motion as a shield, terrified that if she ever stopped, if she ever sat still for a single moment, she would have to face the vast, terrifying emptiness inside her.

She had called it survival. She had not called it what it was.

It was fear. Pure, unadulterated fear.

She gripped the stone tighter. It pulled at her arm, threatening to drag her down into the damp earth. It asked to be left behind. It asked to be put back down, hidden away in the dark center of the woods where no one had to look at it, just as the village hid its own fear.

Maren’s jaw tightened. She took a breath, planted her boots firmly in the dirt, and picked the stone up with both hands.

It was very heavy. She held it against her chest for a long moment, feeling the undeniable, anchoring weight of it. She did not try to deny what it was anymore. She accepted it.

Slowly, deliberately, she lowered the stone into her leather bag. She adjusted the strap over her shoulder, feeling the sudden, dragging burden of it against her hip. She stood up, brushing the dirt from her knees.

She turned to look at Cael and Oswin. She did not explain what she had just felt. She did not offer a profound revelation. She merely adjusted her cloak.

"Let's go back," she said.

The return walk was fundamentally different.

The oppressive atmosphere of the Deep had not vanished—the darkness was still absolute, the shadows still long and sharp—but the quality of their movement had changed. They were no longer walking into the unknown, bracing for an impact that would never come. They were walking toward the edge. They were moving toward something, rather than away from it.

When they reached the edge of the treeline, the village of Drest was still asleep, locked tightly in its self-imposed prison of shuttered windows and barred doors.

Maren stopped at the offering post.

Cael and Oswin paused a few paces behind her. They watched as Maren stepped up to the carved wooden pillar. Without a word of explanation, she reached up and began taking everything off it. She pulled down the woven cloth ribbons, unhooked the small, carved wooden talismans, and gathered the baskets of dried fruit and grain.

She worked with the same methodical efficiency she used to clean a kitchen. She took every single offering, every symbol of the village’s carefully maintained terror, and stuffed them into her heavy pack alongside the Shard of Stone.

She did not say why she was doing it. Cael and Oswin watched her in silence. It was not a question of whether to ask her what she was doing. They understood, with the sudden clarity of people who had just walked through a nightmare and realized they were awake, that there was nothing to ask.

She finished clearing the post, leaving it bare and stripped in the dark. She turned and walked back toward the inn.

They did not stay for morning.

Before the first hints of dawn could turn the eastern sky grey, they were gone. They slipped out of the village while it was still deeply dark, before the elders could wake to find the empty post, before anyone in Drest had reason to look at them differently, or ask questions they were not prepared to answer.

They walked north, taking the road toward Solen's Crossing. The air grew cooler, sharper, shedding the stagnant dread of the valley.

As they walked, the rhythm of their boots striking the hard-packed dirt steady and even, Cael began to hum. It was a low, melodic vibration in the back of his throat. He was not thinking about the song. It was _The Weight of Three_, an old ballad he hadn't performed in years. The melody simply surfaced, rising unbidden to fill the quiet air, a natural consequence of something inside him finally settling into place.

Oswin, walking a few paces behind the bard, heard the tune. He paused for a fraction of a second, the heavy history tome bumping against his side. He reached into his satchel, pulling out his leather-bound journal and a stick of charcoal.

By the faint, pearlescent grey light just beginning to bleed into the morning sky, Oswin wrote a single line in the margins of his notes:

_The bard hums when something has been resolved._

He marked the sentence with a small, precise asterisk, closed the book, and hurried his steps to catch up, his eyes fixed firmly on the road ahead.
