# Chapter 5

<!-- trama:center:end -->

The journey north from the Hollow of Drest took five days of hard walking. The dense, suffocating canopy of the deep woods gradually gave way to rolling, sunlit plains and the wide, muddy expanse of the Solen River. For the three travelers, the distance served as a slow decompression—a necessary buffer between the quiet terror of the village and the noise of civilization.

They arrived in Solen’s Crossing on a Tuesday afternoon, and it became immediately apparent that the town had no interest in being anything other than a monument to itself.

Built on both banks of the broad, slow-moving Solen River, the town was prosperous in a heavy, deliberate way. The architecture in the older quarters featured thick stone facades and ornamental cornices, designed by people who wanted visitors to know exactly how much they could afford to spend on unnecessary masonry. The great stone bridge that stitched the two halves of the settlement together was wide enough for two trade carts to pass abreast, its cobbled surface worn smooth by centuries of commerce. At its center stood a statue of Aldric the Warden, hand raised toward the north in a gesture of eternal, stony defiance.

It was the week of the Aldric Festival, and the town was dressed for the occasion. Banners of crimson and gold cloth draped from the low carved parapets of the bridge and fluttered from the eaves of the merchant houses. The streets smelled of roasting meats, spiced wine, and the sweet, heavy smoke of festival fires.

The atmosphere was warm, aggressively welcoming, and completely convinced of its own narrative.

Cael stopped at the edge of the main square before Maren or Oswin had fully gotten their bearings. A dense crowd of perhaps three hundred people had gathered in a loose semicircle around a raised wooden platform. A traveling theatrical company was in the middle of a performance, staging the town’s founding myth to an audience that knew every beat of the story before the actors even opened their mouths.

Cael watched. He did not watch with the idle curiosity of a festival-goer. He watched with the narrowed, calculating attention of a professional evaluating a competitor.

On stage, a man in polished, overly bright armor was delivering a monologue about holding the river against a horde of northern invaders. The actor was competent. He had a strong baritone that carried well over the ambient noise of the square, and he knew how to plant his feet to make himself look larger. The choreography that followed was enthusiastic, involving a great deal of shouting and the rhythmic clatter of blunted tourney swords.

When the actor portraying Aldric struck down the invader king, forcing him to sign a peace treaty at swordpoint, the crowd erupted into genuine, full-throated cheers. It was a story about absolute moral clarity. The hero was entirely good; the enemy was entirely wicked; the violence was entirely justified, resulting in the great bridge that now stood as a testament to their victory.

Cael stood very still, his arms crossed over his chest. He knew this story.

He did not merely know of it as a piece of regional folklore; he knew its internal mechanics. Two years ago, in a wealthy merchant town a week’s ride south of here, Cael had performed a variation of this exact legend. He had stood in front of a packed guildhall and spun the tale of Aldric the Warden. In fact, Cael had given the story a stronger ending. He had smoothed out the clunky historical pacing and composed a driving, percussive melody for the bridge-building sequence that made the laborers sound less like weary masons and more like an army of the righteous.

The audience had loved it. They had wept, they had cheered, and they had paid him exceedingly well. He had eaten roast duck for three days on the coin he made from that performance.

He glanced to his left, where Maren was watching a juggler at the periphery of the crowd, and to his right, where Oswin was already consulting his satchel of maps. Cael felt a sudden, sharp twist of discomfort in his chest. He did not tell them. He kept his mouth shut, turned his eyes back to the stage, and let the applause of three hundred people wash over him, a sound he suddenly found grating.

While Cael subjected himself to the remainder of the play, Oswin led the way to the Archive of Solen.

According to the records of his scriptorium back in the capital, the second relic they sought—the Still Mirror—was held here. The Archive occupied a handsome two-story building on the west bank, three streets back from the river and comfortably insulated from the noise of the festival. Inside, it smelled of dry parchment, beeswax, and quiet preservation.

The building was clearly maintained by someone who cared deeply about the physical state of the materials, if not necessarily their contents. The archivist on duty introduced himself as Pell. He was a precise, thin-wristed man whose robes were immaculate and whose tone suggested he considered himself the guardian of the town’s civic soul. He had held the post for nineteen years, a fact he managed to convey within the first three minutes of their conversation.

Pell welcomed Oswin with the cautious professional courtesy archivists reserve for visiting scholars. He provided the official index, a leather-bound ledger detailing the contents of the ground floor, and offered a remarkably thorough orientation.

"The census records are in the north wing, categorized by decade," Pell explained, gesturing with a long, clean finger. "Trade agreements and guild charters are housed in the east wing, naturally. And the official commemorative texts detailing the Warden’s victory and the subsequent bridge commissions are displayed in the central gallery. I trust you will find our methodology satisfactory."

"It is very orderly," Oswin said, laying his own journal flat on a reading table.

For the next hour, Oswin worked his way through the index methodically. He did not skim. He cross-referenced the shelf markers with the official map Pell had provided. Everything matched, precisely as the archivist had claimed, until Oswin’s finger stopped on a greyed-out rectangle near the rear of the building’s diagram.

Oswin approached the front desk, where Pell was carefully trimming the wick of a reading lantern.

"Excuse me," Oswin said, keeping his tone perfectly neutral. "The official map indicates a structural annex here, extending beneath the ground floor stacks. I don't see an entry for it in the index."

Pell did not flinch, but the rhythm of his scissors paused for just a fraction of a second. He set the tool down and folded his hands on the desk.

"Ah, the annex," Pell said, his voice dropping into a register of practiced, regretful authority. "Yes. That area has been sealed for several years, I'm afraid. We had some rather severe structural concerns regarding the load-bearing masonry near the river-facing foundation. Dampness, you understand. The risk of rot to the timbers was deemed too high, and the structural integrity was compromised. All materials of any historical or research value were relocated to the upper galleries long before my tenure began. There is really nothing down there of interest to a researcher, merely empty shelving and compromised stonework. It is entirely inaccessible."

"I see," Oswin said. "Thank you."

Oswin returned to his reading table. He opened his journal. He wrote down Pell’s explanation regarding the structural concerns and the relocated materials verbatim. He did not underline it. He did not add an exclamation point.

Instead, in the margin beside the entry, Oswin wrote, in his neat, cramped script: _His answer to my question about the annex was approximately twice as long as his answers to every other question._

He drew no grand conclusions. He did not construct a theory of conspiracy. He was a scribe, and his job was to record what happened. The anomaly was noted; the rest would wait.

Maren had not accompanied them to the Archive, nor had she stayed in the square to watch the armor-clad actors shout at each other. Maren had no particular interest in official histories, whether they were shouted from a stage or bound in leather. She was, however, deeply interested in how places actually functioned.

She spent the afternoon in the market. The festival had drawn merchants from across the region, turning the streets near the bridge into a dense, chaotic weave of stalls, awnings, and haggling voices. Maren moved through the crowd with the steady, unimpeded grace of a woman who had spent half her life navigating crowded bakeries and busy thoroughfares.

Near the entrance to the bridge, she purchased a loaf of dark, seed-crusted bread from a vendor whose hands were dusted with coarse rye flour. She tore off a piece, tasted it, and stopped walking. It was exceptional. The crust shattered perfectly against the teeth, giving way to a crumb that was dense, slightly sour, and deeply flavorful. Later, she would describe it to Cael and Oswin as the best thing she had eaten since leaving the capital—a simple, factual observation that Cael would somehow take as a personal reproach.

For now, she leaned against the stone parapet, eating the bread and talking to the vendor. Maren possessed a rare quality: she knew how to listen to working people in a way that made them want to talk. She did not ask about Aldric the Warden or the glorious history of the bridge. She asked about the price of flour, the river tolls, and who owned the barges moored on the east bank.

Over the course of three hours, speaking with the baker, a woman selling pickled fish, and a man repairing cart-wheels, Maren learned more about the actual history of Solen’s Crossing than Oswin would ever find in the upper galleries of the Archive.

She learned that the town’s prosperity was largely built on a truce between two merchant families—the Vanes and the Cortrells—who had spent twenty years engaged in a vicious, localized trade war before the bridge was built. She learned that the current mayor was a Vane, but the harbor master was a Cortrell, and that the festival was less a celebration of a mythical hero and more an annual, highly orchestrated display of diplomatic posturing between the two factions. The grievances were not historical; they were active, simmering just beneath the colorful cloth and the spiced wine.

Maren did not immediately connect any of this to the Demon King, the relics, or the grand quest the king had sent them on. She merely filed it away. She liked to know the shape of the ground she was standing on.

That evening, the festival began to wind down in the streets, but the taverns grew loud and crowded. Cael returned to the square alone.

He found the theatrical company’s lead actor in a tavern called The Broken Oar, sitting in a corner booth with a mug of dark ale. The man looked tired, his face scrubbed free of stage paint, but he carried the distinct, lingering satisfaction of an actor who knew he had held an audience in the palm of his hand.

Cael bought a pitcher of the tavern’s best ale and slid into the booth opposite him.

He was good at this. Cael knew how to speak the language of the trade. For two hours, they engaged in professional shop-talk. Cael complimented the man’s breath control during the final monologue, praised the pacing of the sword choreography, and asked intelligent, specific questions about how they managed the acoustics in an open square. The actor, flattered to be recognized by a peer, expanded, loosened up, and drank generously from the pitcher.

When the mood was thoroughly relaxed, Cael leaned forward, tracing the grain of the wooden table with his thumb.

"The script you use," Cael said casually. "It’s tight. The pacing is excellent. I’m curious, though—when the company was putting it together, did anyone ever look into the actual founding records? Did you research the original documents in the Archive before writing it?"

The actor paused, his mug halfway to his mouth. He looked at Cael as if he had just asked why the sky wasn't green.

He let out a short, booming laugh. "The original records? Gods, why would we do that?"

"For accuracy?" Cael suggested, keeping his tone light.

"Accuracy." The actor scoffed, taking a long pull of ale and wiping his mouth with the back of his hand. "Friend, we used the official commemorative pamphlet. It has everything you need. The hero, the villain, the sword, the bridge. The story is completely established. Everyone knows how Solen was founded."

"Right," Cael said quietly.

"Why would anyone look further?" The actor asked, gesturing broadly around the crowded tavern. "Look at them. They don't want a history lesson. They want Aldric the Warden. You start digging through dusty ledgers, you just end up with a bunch of boring merchants arguing over river tolls. Where's the drama in that? Nobody pays copper to see a trade dispute."

Cael stared at the man for a long moment. He saw the easy, unbothered confidence in the actor's eyes. It was a mirror reflecting his own face from two years ago, standing in a guildhall a week to the south, prioritizing a good melody over the truth because it paid well.

"You're right," Cael said, his voice flat. "Everyone knows the story."

He thanked the actor for the conversation, left a silver coin on the table to cover the pitcher, and walked out into the cool night air.

The walk back to the inn was solitary and quiet. The colored lanterns were still lit, casting wavering, festive pools of light across the cobblestones. The crowd had thinned to a few straggling revelers and sleepy merchants closing up their stalls.

Cael stopped at the entrance to the bridge. He looked up at the statue of Aldric the Warden, its stone hand raised in false defiance. The bridge was hung with cloth honoring a man who, if the whispers Cael was beginning to hear were true, had merely mediated a commercial dispute. The town had dressed a bureaucrat in armor and called him a savior because it made for better business.

And Cael was implicated. He had not merely observed this deception; he was a participant. He had taken the lie, polished it, set it to music, and sold it. For money. To people who had left his performance believing it as absolute truth.

What does it feel like to stand in the middle of a story you have told, knowing it isn't true?

It felt like a physical weight settling into his bones. It felt like being caught in a trap he had helped build.

Cael walked through the lantern light back to the inn. He was in a far worse mood than when he had left, and when he finally reached the room he shared with Oswin, he did not, for once, have a single word to say.
