# Chapter 10 — The Dungeon

The cell was a masterclass in deprivation. It was cold, a deep, unyielding dampness that seemed to seep outward from the rough-hewn stone itself rather than inward from the air. High above, near the vaulted ceiling, a single grated window let in a thin, precise rectangle of light. For the first few hours of their captivity, they had done nothing but watch that pale geometry crawl across the uneven floorboards, charting the slow, indifferent progression of the afternoon. It was the only movement in the room. There was no furniture, no straw, no bucket. There was nothing but the heavy iron door, its hinges thick with ancient grease, and the silence of a fortress that did not care if they existed.

They had been in the cell for approximately half a day when Maren opened her satchel. The leather creaked, a loud sound in the absolute stillness. She drew out the remaining loaf of bread—slightly stale now, its crust dusted with the flour of three different towns they had passed through before the world turned entirely to ash and stone. She broke it into three pieces. She handed one to Cael, one to Oswin, and kept the smallest for herself.

She did this without comment. There was no discussion of rationing, no fearful talk of how long they might be kept there to rot. The bread was divided because they were hungry now, and Maren lived exclusively in the reality of the present.

"So," Maren said. She leaned her back against the weeping wall, pulling her knees up to her chest to preserve warmth. "What did we miss?"

It was the exact question she had asked when the heavy door had first locked behind them with a definitive, ringing thud. Now, with the quiet settling around them like a suffocating blanket, it was the only question left to answer.

Oswin unbuckled his pack. His hands, usually so precise and deliberate, were trembling slightly. He noticed it, frowned, and forced them to steady as he drew out his leather-bound journal. The ink on his fingers had long since dried into the deep creases of his skin, permanent stains of a profession he suddenly felt very far away from.

"We go back to the beginning," Oswin said, his voice surprisingly firm in the echoing, cavernous space. "We list what we saw. What we were told. What we assumed."

They began the inventory. It was not a fast process. Oswin’s influence shaped it—methodical, unhurried, insisting on absolute clarity over the comfort of easy answers. He opened his journal to the very first entry, the ink dark and sharp from a time when the quest had seemed theoretical, a mere transcription task assigned by an abbot.

For hours, they systematically dismantled their journey. The afternoon dragged on, the rectangle of light stretching and warping against the far wall.

Maren was ruthlessly honest about what she had assumed versus what she had actually observed. She admitted, her voice flat and factual, that she had initially viewed the trials as straightforward physical obstacles. Things to be survived, endured, or outsmarted. She had assumed the magic they sought resided within the objects themselves—because that was how the stories always worked. A glowing sword, a heavy shield, a ring of power. 

"I expected a transaction," she said, tearing off a small piece of bread. "We bleed a little, we get a weapon. I didn't question it because I didn't want to think about what happens if the stories are wrong."

Cael, pacing the short, six-step length of the cell, was slower to speak. He kept his eyes on the floor, measuring his strides. When he finally contributed, he offered the pieces he had deliberately filed away and ignored. He spoke of the king at the palace.

"It wasn't just what he said," Cael murmured, stopping his pacing to lean against the cold iron bars of the door. "It was how he looked. The velvet of his robes was perfect, but he was sweating. The throne room was freezing, and he was sweating. And he spoke of the Demon King... not with righteous fury. Not the way a father speaks of the monster who murdered his son. He spoke with a strange, suffocating despair. Like a man reading a ledger of debts he knows he can never pay." Cael rubbed his face. "I noticed it. I recorded it. And I chose not to examine it, because it contradicted the role I was supposed to play. A hero doesn't question the king's grief."

The inventory was the group functioning at its absolute fullest. Stripped of the road’s constant urgency, locked in a cell with nothing to do but think, their minds sharpened against one another like flint striking steel.

By the time the rectangle of light on the floor faded into a bruised, twilight purple, casting long, wavering shadows across their faces, four distinct anomalies sat unresolved between them.

The first was a memory of Cael’s, unearthed from the very beginning of the ordeal. During their private audience in the palace, before any formal oaths were sworn, the king had asked Cael specifically about an old ballad called *The Weight of Three*. The king had never explained why. He had simply asked if Cael knew it. At the time, Cael, overwhelmed by the royal presence and the weight of the summons, had forgotten the question entirely within the hour. He had not forgotten it now. It burned in his mind like a stray spark.

The second anomaly was the ballad itself. Throughout the journey, in quiet moments around the dying embers of a campfire or while walking through the silent, oppressive woods, Cael had been humming fragments of a tune. Oswin had noted it in his journal—two separate entries marked with asterisks tracking the occurrence, rigorous in his documentation of the bard's habits. Cael had done it unconsciously, unable to explain why that specific melody kept surfacing when he was stressed or afraid. The structure of *The Weight of Three* was unlike anything else in the entire bardic tradition. Its verses described three people carrying unnamed burdens, and three objects that did not grant any power, but merely illuminated what the holder was already carrying. It was a deeply unsatisfying song to perform. It lacked a climax. It lacked a victory.

The third was the marginal annotation. It had haunted Oswin’s records, a ghost in the ink. They had seen it three times now: first in the manuscript Oswin had painstakingly copied at his own scriptorium; then in the commercial archive in the bustling city of Solen; and finally, undeniably, on the original, crumbling text at the monastery. The monastery visit had revealed the crucial, indisputable detail: the annotation had been added later. The ink was a different chemical composition; the hand was entirely different from the original scribe's flowing script. Someone had placed it there deliberately, an intentional intrusion upon a sacred text.

The fourth anomaly was the quietest, but perhaps the most unnerving. Oswin reached into his deep wool pocket and drew out the Iron Key. The cell was freezing, their breath pluming faintly in the dim air with every exhalation. But when Oswin passed the heavy iron key to Maren, she did not flinch from the cold metal.

The key was warm.

It had been warm since Oswin first picked it up in the Abbot's pristine study during the third trial. It pulsed with a steady, blood-temperature heat that the freezing dungeon, the stone walls, and the subterranean chill could not extinguish. It felt alive.

"Sing the ballad," Oswin said, looking up from his notes, the pages covered in his dense, cramped handwriting. "Not the pieces. The whole thing. From beginning to end."

Cael stopped his restless pacing. He stood in the center of the cell, closing his eyes, letting the darkness behind his eyelids center him. He had never performed *The Weight of Three* for an audience. He had kept it in his repertoire only because its structural wrongness bothered his professional sensibilities, a musical puzzle he could not solve.

He began to sing.

In the damp, echoing acoustic of the dungeon, his voice was clear, resonant, and entirely stripped of a performer’s artificial flourish. He did not sing to entertain. He sang to examine.

He sang the first verse. It described a person carrying a grief so old, so calcified, it had become the very shape of their spine. They encountered an object—a stone. And the stone did not take the grief away. It offered no magical relief. It simply showed them that the grief was theirs to set down, if they chose. It illuminated the burden without lifting it.

He sang the second verse. A person carrying a story they no longer believed, a lie they told daily to keep the world predictable and safe. They found a silver mirror. The mirror did not show them the objective truth of the universe. It merely reflected the sheer exhaustion of their own deception. It showed them the cost of the lie.

He sang the third verse. A person carrying a vow made for someone else’s reasons, a duty that had hollowed them out until they were nothing but an echo of someone else's will. They were handed an iron key, described in the song as an *iron crown upon the hollow shore*. The key opened no literal doors. It only showed them that the cage they stood in had never been locked.

Cael paused. In each verse, the object changed nothing about the world. The verse ended. But the person holding the object was different.

"There's a fourth verse," Cael said softly into the silence. "I always thought of it as an awkward appendix. A later addition by a less skilled poet. The melody shifts into a minor key. The language is older, clumsier. It doesn't rhyme properly."

"Sing it," Maren said, her eyes fixed on him in the gloom.

Cael took a deep breath, letting the rhythm change in his mind, leaning into the older, harsher cadence. The fourth verse described a moment of impossible convergence. It stated that when someone who had seen all three objects finally stood before the throne of the usurper, they must speak the name of the one trapped inside the one who rules. The name had to be spoken not as a weapon, not as a curse, and not directed at the demon itself. It had to be spoken through the monster. It had to be aimed, with precision and profound empathy, at whoever was still alive underneath the possession.

Cael finished. The last note hung in the air, vibrating slightly against the stone before fading completely. The silence in the cell was absolute.

Oswin frantically turned the pages of his journal, the vellum crisp and loud in the quiet. He found the page he was looking for, his finger tracing down the margin.

"The annotation," Oswin said. His voice was a dry rasp. He cleared his throat and read the eleven words aloud. They were in the old dialect, rough and phonetic, devoid of poetic meter.

Cael went very still. The temperature in the room seemed to drop further, yet his skin prickled. He looked at Oswin, then at Maren.

The annotation was the fourth verse.

It was compressed, aged, stripped of melody and set in a dialect that predated the song’s current form by centuries. But the intellectual content was identical. The instruction was exactly the same. The underlying structure held perfectly.

Someone who had understood what the legend actually meant—someone who knew that the relics were not weapons but lenses—had added the fourth verse of *The Weight of Three* to the margins of the holy manuscript. And then, someone—perhaps the same scholar, perhaps a dedicated disciple—had placed a copy of that exact note in a commercial archive in Solen three hundred years ago.

"Two preservation paths," Oswin whispered, his eyes wide as he stared at his own handwriting, seeing it truly for the first time. "Someone built two separate, distinct paths for the very same piece of information. Different forms, different locations. One hidden in a song for the common folk, one hidden in the margins of a text for the scholars. So that it would eventually reach whoever finally understood the rest of it."

"Who wrote it?" Cael asked, his voice hushed.

Nobody knew. The centuries had swallowed the name, leaving only the intent.

"The question of who wrote it," Maren said, her voice cutting sharply through the rising tide of academic awe, "is separate from the question of whether it is useful."

She looked between them, her gaze sharp, piercing, refusing to let them drift into intellectual wonder. She was the anchor pulling them back to the cold stone floor of a locked cell.

The method was now painfully clear, laid bare when the fourth verse and the ancient annotation were placed side by side. To break the possession, they had to speak the prince’s true name. They had to use it as an address, aimed past the terrifying, demonic entity at the human being still buried inside the shell of the body. Not an accusation. Not a magical incantation meant to burn or bind. A simple call. A recognition.

But the ballad was explicit about the psychological conditions required. This act could not be done by someone who was actively afraid. It could not be done by someone operating from a false, comforting story about what was happening. It could not be done by someone waiting for a king’s permission, or a god's blessing, to act.

The trials, they realized with a collective, sickening drop in their stomachs, were never requirements for collecting magical weapons. The Demon King had proved that emphatically by tossing the relics casually back into Cael's satchel and locking them all in the dungeon. The relics meant nothing to him. The King in Valdenmere hadn't sent them to arm a hero; he had sent them to gather the dangerous, symbolic artifacts that threatened his lie, using Cael as an expendable courier to deliver them directly to the demon so they could be locked away forever.

But the King had misunderstood the legend. The trials were requirements for becoming the kind of people who could stand in that throne room and do what needed to be done without breaking. The relics were just the curriculum. The heroes were the weapon.

"The prince's name," Maren said, breaking the heavy silence. "Do we know it?"

Cael frowned, searching his memory, digging through the panic and awe of that first day in the capital. "The king said it. Once. During the palace meeting, when he was pacing, when he thought I wasn't listening closely enough to the details. I didn't write it down. I think... I'm nearly certain I remember it."

"I wrote it down," Oswin said immediately.

He flipped backward through the journal, past the trials, past the miles of road, to the very first pages, written when his hands had not yet known the grip of fear. His ink-stained finger traced down a column of dense, neat script. It stopped.

"Corvin," Oswin read. He looked up, his eyes meeting Cael's. "His name is Corvin."

The name hung in the damp air, a small, fragile, human sound in a fortress built by and for monsters.

Then Maren asked the question.

She didn't ask if they were strong enough to face the monster again. She didn't ask how they would get past the heavily armed guards outside the heavy iron door. She looked directly at Cael, and then at Oswin, and she asked the only question that actually mattered. The question that defined everything.

"Can we actually do what we think we now understand?" Maren asked quietly, her voice devoid of inflection. "Or are we just telling ourselves a story, because it's the only story available to us right now?"

Nobody spoke for a long, agonizing moment.

It was the question that ruthlessly separated understanding from performance. It was remarkably easy to sit in a locked cell, safe from immediate harm, and decode an ancient puzzle. It was deeply satisfying to feel the pieces clicking together, to solve the riddle. It was entirely another thing to believe that intellectual solution enough to bet their actual lives on it when they walked back into the throne room with empty hands and faced a creature of nightmare.

Cael looked down at his own hands. They were the hands of a bard. They were calloused from the tight tension of lute strings, not from the heavy hilt of swords. He thought of the king's terror, the lies that had built a kingdom. He thought of the old grief in the first verse, the false story in the second, the surrendered will in the third.

He had carried all of them. They all had. And they had survived the seeing of them.

"I don't know," Cael said finally. His voice was remarkably steady, stripped of all theatricality. "But I know what the alternative is."

He looked up at the high window. The thin rectangle of light had vanished completely. The patch of sky visible through the grate had gone from a bruised purple to a slightly different, heavier shade of grey. Night was coming, or had already come. The fortress around them was totally silent, indifferent to their revelations.

Maren followed his gaze. She looked at the immovable iron door, then back to Cael and Oswin. Her expression did not soften, but the set of her jaw hardened into something resembling forged iron.

"Then we should be ready before morning," Maren said.
