# Chapter 1 — The Summons

The Gilded Post was the sort of establishment that charged double for performers who drew a crowd and triple for those who did not. Cael, currently midway through a ballad about a merchant who accidentally married a river spirit, was firmly on the triple rate.

The common room was only half-full. The air smelled of spilled cider, woodsmoke, and the damp wool of patrons who had spent the afternoon working in the river-chill. They were a moderate audience, neither entirely hostile nor entirely captivated, offering the kind of distracted amusement that meant they would remember the melody but forget the singer before they woke up the next morning.

Cael did not mind. He was not there to be remembered; he was there to delay his eviction. He plucked the strings of his lute, letting the familiar cadence carry him through the fifth verse. He watched the room as he sang, his face composed, his voice projecting the tragic longing of the merchant without feeling a trace of it himself. Cael had learned a long time ago that you did not need to believe a story to give it its proper shape. You only needed to know where the pauses went.

He was just reaching the bridge of the song—where the river spirit demands her toll—when the heavy oak door of the inn groaned open.

Two men stepped inside. They were dressed in the boiled leather and steel trim of the King’s Guard. The effect on the room was immediate. Conversations dropped from boisterous arguments to hushed murmurs. Tankards were set down gently. In a city like Valdenmere, the Guard did not visit mid-grade taverns in the trades district to enjoy the music.

The soldiers scanned the room, their eyes moving past the merchants and the river-workers, before settling definitively on the small stage in the corner. They started walking toward him.

Cael saw them. He saw the tension in the room, the sudden tightening of the landlord's jaw near the bar. But there were four lines left in the verse, and stopping early would ruin the meter. It was either a matter of strict professionalism or sheer, quiet stubbornness—even Cael wasn't entirely sure which—but he did not stop playing. He held the guards’ gaze and delivered the final, resonant notes of the merchant’s doom, letting the chord fade naturally into the silence of the room.

Only then did he mute the strings with his palm.

"Cael?" the taller of the two guards asked. It wasn't really a question.

"I am," Cael said.

"The King requires your presence. You are to come with us."

They did not offer an explanation, and Cael did not ask for one. He simply unlooped his instrument's strap, set it carefully in its battered leather case, and followed them out into the cold night.

The walk through Valdenmere gave the impression of a city that had long ago decided what it was and saw no reason to change.

The streets were wide near the market but narrowed unpredictably as they moved closer to the inner ring, following paths that had been dirt cart tracks a thousand years before. Valdenmere was not a beautiful city. It was a place of accumulated function. Neighborhoods swallowed other neighborhoods, walls were built against older walls, and the stones underfoot were smoothed by the passage of millions of forgotten errands. There was a weight to the capital, a profound indifference to the people living inside it. It felt like a city that had already decided what was true and was just getting on with the business of existing.

The guards walked in silence. Cael matched their pace, his instrument strapped securely across his back.

Eventually, the palace loomed out of the darkness, occupying the northern quarter of the city’s interior ring. From a distance, especially from the lower districts, it looked like a structure of myth—soaring spires, unbreachable walls, a monument to the kingdom's unbroken continuity. But as they approached the gates, Cael noticed the reality of it. The stonework was older than the current dynasty. There were patch jobs along the lower parapets where the mortar didn't quite match. It was heavily renovated, functional, and deeply tired.

They bypassed the main thoroughfare and entered through a side gate, moving down a series of quiet, drafty corridors lit by sputtering sconces.

Cael had expected to be thrown in a cell, or perhaps dragged into the great throne room to face an intimidating assembly of nobles. Instead, the guards halted before a modest oak door, knocked once, and pushed it open, gesturing for him to enter alone.

The receiving room was small, warmed by a modest hearth and lit by two oil lamps that cast long, steady shadows across the stone floor. There were no tapestries, no gilded chairs. Against the far wall sat an advisor in dark robes, half-swallowed by the gloom, holding a quill over a parchment.

And behind a simple wooden table sat King Aldren.

The King of Valdenmere did not look like the towering figure stamped on the kingdom's coins. He was a man in his fifties, wearing a plain, high-collared tunic. His face was lined with the administrative exhaustion of a man who had spent twenty-three years keeping things from becoming worse. He looked competent, practical, and heavily burdened by the public grief of a father who had lost his only son to a monster.

There was a document already resting on the table between them.

"Sit," the king said. His voice was calm, pitched for a small room.

Cael sat in the straight-backed chair opposite the table. He did not speak. He had learned that when powerful people summon you, it is usually best to let them spend the first words.

"You are Cael," the king said, studying him. "You travel the northern circuit. You know the old songs."

"I know the songs people pay to hear, Your Majesty."

Aldren offered a faint, dry smile that didn't reach his eyes. "I will be brief. The Demon King occupies the fortress beyond the northern border. You know this. You know the stories of what he is, what he has done."

Cael nodded slowly. Everyone knew the official history. The monster at the border. The murdered prince. The tragedy that had defined Aldren’s reign.

"There is an old prophecy," Aldren continued, his tone turning clinical, as if he were reciting a trade manifest. "It speaks of a time when the Demon King's power might be broken. It requires three ancient relics, gathered from three hidden trials. And it states that the one to gather them must be 'a traveler of songs'."

The king paused, letting the words hang in the quiet room. The advisor in the corner did not make a sound, but Cael could hear the faint scratch of the quill.

"I see," Cael said carefully.

"The scholars have debated the text for years. I have grown tired of debate. The prophecy is specific enough to act upon, and vague enough to mean anyone. You are a traveler of songs. You will seek the relics."

Cael looked at the king, then down at the document on the table, and then back up. He felt a profound sense of absurdity. He was a performer who was two silver coins away from sleeping in an alley, and he was being handed a myth.

"Your Majesty," Cael said, keeping his voice respectful but entirely flat. "I am a bard. I cut bread with my knife, not throats. I have sung the legend of the three relics in half a hundred taverns, and there are at least four different versions of it, none of which agree on where the relics actually are. Prophecies are... flexible. They are shaped for the audience."

Aldren did not look angry. He did not look insulted. He merely looked like a man who had anticipated this exact objection.

"I am not asking you to believe the story," the king said quietly. "Belief is not required. Action is. If the prophecy is false, you will be well compensated for your time. If it is true, you will have done the kingdom a service no army could achieve."

The king pushed the document across the table. Next to it, he placed a heavy leather purse that clinked with the undeniable sound of gold, and a folded parchment bearing the royal seal.

"A letter of passage," Aldren said. "Provisions. Funding. And the formal blessing of the crown. All you must do is sign the writ of commission."

Cael looked at the paper. He did not rush. The silence in the room stretched out, thick and heavy, but nobody interrupted him. He read the precise, legalistic handwriting of the writ. It promised exactly what the king said it promised. It bound Cael to head north, to seek the trials, to claim the relics. It was a death sentence written in very polite calligraphy.

But it was also a way out of the Gilded Post. And, beneath his practiced detachment, Cael felt a faint, uncharacteristic stirring of curiosity. He had spent his entire life repeating stories other people had lived. Now, the architecture of a story had opened its door and invited him inside.

He picked up the quill. He signed his name at the bottom of the page.

Aldren watched him do it. For a fraction of a second, the crushing administrative weariness on the king’s face seemed to fracture, revealing an expression that Cael could only describe as hope. Or perhaps desperation. It was gone before Cael could be sure.

"It is done," Aldren said. He pulled the document back.

Cael stood up, taking the purse and the letter of passage. He bowed, a shallow, practiced performer's bow, and turned toward the door.

"One moment," the king said.

Cael stopped.

"Do you know an old ballad," Aldren asked, his voice suddenly very casual, "called _The Weight of Three_?"

Cael frowned, looking back over his shoulder. It was an incredibly obscure question. "I do, Your Majesty. Though I rarely perform it."

"Why not?"

"The melody is awkward," Cael explained, his critical instincts taking over for a moment. "It doesn't flow naturally. And the verses don't resolve in a way that audiences find satisfying. They prefer stories that close their loops. _The Weight of Three_ just... ends."

Aldren held his gaze for a long moment. Then he nodded.

"I see. Thank you, Cael. That will be all."

In the corner of the room, the advisor’s quill scratched loudly against the parchment. Cael pushed the door open and stepped back into the corridor. He forgot the exchange almost immediately.

That night, in his cramped room at the Gilded Post, Cael packed his belongings.

It did not take long. He possessed the pragmatic efficiency of someone who had packed in a hurry more times than he cared to count. He laid out his spare tunic, his whetstone, and his small, practical knife—a blade meant for slicing cheese and apples, not for fending off monsters. He placed the heavy royal purse at the bottom of his pack. He carefully secured his lute inside its reinforced, rigid leather case—a heavy, battered piece of travel gear specifically designed to survive being thrown from a moving cart or dropped in a ditch. He checked the iron clasps twice, ensuring the instrument was safe from the damp and the road.

He sat on the edge of the narrow bed and looked at the closed window.

Most people, upon being sent on a potentially fatal quest by the crown, might write a letter home. They might visit a temple, or seek out a friend to share a final drink. Cael did none of these things. He had no home to write to; the farming village he had left years ago was just another place he no longer belonged. He had no friends in Valdenmere, only landlords and transient audiences.

He blew out the candle and lay down in the dark.

He was not afraid, exactly. Fear required a clear understanding of the threat, and the 'Demon King' was still just a character from a song to him, a shape made of words. But as he stared at the ceiling, Cael was acutely aware that something fundamental had shifted. The static, predictable rhythm of his life had been broken.

He wasn't sure yet if the change was going to be interesting, or simply very inconvenient. He closed his eyes, and slept.

The morning air was biting and crisp. Valdenmere was waking up slowly as Cael approached the north gate.

The heavy iron portcullis was already raised. The guards on duty were huddled around a small brazier, passing a pair of worn wooden dice between them. They did not look up from their game as Cael walked past. They saw fifty people leave every morning—merchants, messengers, wanderers. To them, he was just another silhouette slipping out into the dawn.

Cael adjusted the strap of his instrument across his back, patted the knife at his belt, and stepped through the archway. He did not look back.

The city did not see him go.
